The Sign of the Four — Deducibility Analysis

Basic Information

  • Author: Arthur Conan Doyle
  • Year: 1890
  • Type: Novel
  • Detective: Sherlock Holmes, assisted by Dr. John Watson
  • Core mystery type: whodunit + howdunit + imperial historical entanglement (treasure hunt)

Deducibility Rating Summary

Metric Stars Description
Deducibility ⭐⭐⭐ Average convergence point 62% — the method of murder and the identity of the primary suspect can be deduced in the first half, but the key reversal — that the one who actually committed the killing was not the mastermind — does not fully emerge until after the 83% mark
Fog Rating ⭐⭐⭐ Moderate misdirection: the false arrest trail laid by the official detective, the suspicious figure of Thaddeus Sholto, and the reader's habitual assumption that "mastermind = killer" together form three layers of calculated deception

Synopsis

In 1878, Captain Arthur Morstan, a British army officer stationed in India, returned to London from Bombay and that same evening visited his old acquaintance Major Sholto. The two men quarrelled fiercely over the distribution of a vast fortune in jewels — the "Agra Treasure" — acquired in India. In the heat of the argument, Morstan suffered a fatal heart attack. Fearing accusation of murder, Sholto concealed the body and kept the treasure for himself. Morstan's daughter Mary never saw her father again.

Six years later, Major Sholto, on his deathbed, confessed everything to his twin sons Thaddeus and Bartholomew. At the very moment he was revealing the hiding place of the treasure, a grim bearded face appeared at the window — that of Jonathan Small, who had escaped from the Andaman Islands prison and had been hunting the treasure for years. The shock killed Sholto before he could speak the final word of his secret.

In 1888, Mary Morstan received anonymous letters and was invited to appear at the Lyceum Theatre. Holmes and Watson accompanied her, and there they met the agitated Thaddeus Sholto, who informed them that his brother Bartholomew had just discovered the treasure in the attic of their home.

But when the party arrived at Pondicherry Lodge, Bartholomew Sholto was already dead — a ghastly grin fixed on his face, a tiny poisoned thorn embedded in his scalp, the treasure gone without a trace, and a note left on the table: "The Sign of the Four."

Holmes read the scene swiftly: marks of a wooden leg, the prints of a bare foot no bigger than a child's, a foreign-made poisoned blow-pipe dart, and rope marks showing entry through the roof. He identified two criminals: the one-legged Jonathan Small and a native companion from the Andaman Islands. The Baker Street Irregulars, a keen-nosed mongrel named Toby, and a sleepless night of pursuit led them to the steam launch Aurora on the Thames.

The nocturnal chase on the river ended in tragedy: when Tonga raised his blow-pipe, Holmes and Watson fired simultaneously, and he fell into the Thames never to surface again. Jonathan Small was captured, but in the moment before he was overtaken he had quietly scattered the treasure into the river. The heavy iron box, when opened, was empty.

At the moment the treasure vanished, Watson spoke his most honest words: "Thank God!" — the barrier of wealth that had stood between him and Mary Morstan was gone at last.

Truth Breakdown

Truth 1: How was Bartholomew Sholto killed? (howdunit)

  • Clue convergence point: approximately 50%
  • Answer: Tonga used a blow-pipe dart of the kind native to the Andaman Islands, firing it downward through an opening in the ceiling into the top of Bartholomew Sholto's head. The dart was coated with a powerful alkaloid poison similar in effect to strychnine, causing muscular tetanic contraction — the "Satanic smile" (risus sardonicus) — and rapid death. Jonathan Small entered via the roof and descended by rope; he was the mastermind behind the removal of the treasure, but he was not the one who committed the killing.

Truth 2: Who is Jonathan Small? (who is the criminal)

  • Clue convergence point: approximately 50%
  • Answer: Jonathan Small is a British man who, during the Indian Mutiny, participated in the robbery of a messenger named Achmet and the seizure of the Agra Treasure, for which he served many years of hard labour in the Andaman Islands. He believes half the treasure is rightfully his by the terms of the "Sign of the Four" pact, and he burns with resentment over the betrayal by Morstan and Sholto. Having made an ally of the Andaman Islander Tonga during his imprisonment, he escaped and tracked the treasure to England. His right leg was bitten off by a crocodile in India and replaced with a wooden leg.

Truth 3: Who actually committed the murder? (the core reversal of the whodunit)

  • Clue convergence point: approximately 85%
  • Answer: Not Jonathan Small, but Tonga. Small's objective was to recover the treasure; he had hoped Bartholomew would not be in the room. But Tonga went ahead, passing through the ceiling opening, and shot Bartholomew with a poisoned dart. Small afterwards "thrashed the little devil with the slack end of the rope" and was deeply grieved by the act.

Truth 4: The final fate of the Agra Treasure (where did it go)

  • Clue convergence point: approximately 92%
  • Answer: Before he was overtaken, Small threw the treasure piece by piece into the Thames. He would sooner let the wealth sink to the river bottom than allow Sholto's or Morstan's descendants to benefit from it. The "Sign of the Four" pact was sacred to him — the treasure belonged to all four of them together, and to no one else.

Reasoning Path Reconstruction

First Path: Deducing the Method of Murder (howdunit)

Readers encounter the death scene in Chapter 5 (approximately 42%): a locked room, a face contorted into a horrible grin, a tiny spine in the scalp. At this point, an attentive reader with basic medical knowledge can deduce "alkaloid poison" alongside Watson, and after Holmes extracts the thorn and examines its "non-British" characteristics, can complete the inference of "murder by poisoned blow-pipe dart."

Chapter 6 (approximately 50%) is the full convergence point for this path: the combination of wooden-leg prints and bare-foot prints, the route of entry through the roof, the use of rope and hook — the method of murder is laid out in full. For the careful reader, the scene can be completely reconstructed at this stage.

Second Path: Deducing the Identity of the Primary Suspect (whodunit — Jonathan Small)

The document in Chapter 3 (25%) is the first piece of the puzzle: "The Sign of the Four," four names, Jonathan Small the only Englishman among them. In Chapter 4 (33%), Thaddeus recounts his father's terror of the one-legged white man, corroborating the document. In Chapters 6–7 (48–58%), Holmes performs an open "display of deduction," walking readers through his entire chain of logic; by this point all the key clues have assembled, and a reader following closely can arrive at the answer in step with Holmes.

Third Path: Deducing the True Killer (whodunit — Tonga, not Small)

This is the deepest and most unexpected reversal. For the first two-thirds of the book, Holmes and reader alike fix their attention on "the wooden-legged Jonathan Small"; the mysterious bare-footed companion exists for a prolonged stretch as little more than an enigma, deliberately kept vague.

The first faint foreshadowing comes in Chapter 7 (55%): Holmes remarks that "the murder looked more like the independent action of the companion, and one which had caused Small vexation" — but most readers, caught up in the excitement of the chase, will glide past this sentence. It is not until Chapter 10 (83%), when Tonga raises his blow-pipe and is shot dead, and Chapter 11 (88%), when Small confesses after his capture, that readers suddenly realise: the small figure who existed throughout the book as little more than an appendage was the true killer all along.

This is Doyle's carefully engineered cognitive displacement: the reader's gaze follows the wooden-legged man throughout, while the more dangerous shadow crouching beside him goes unnoticed.

Fourth Path: The Fate of the Treasure

Almost no reader can guess during the chase in Chapter 10 that the treasure has already been scattered — the visual focus of that scene is entirely on the tension of the pursuit. The revelation of the empty box is a pure reversal with almost no foreshadowing, yet it carries an unexpected emotional release.

Additional Notes

The Place of The Sign of the Four in Conan Doyle's Work

The Sign of the Four is the second Holmes novel, written after A Study in Scarlet (1887); it opens by deliberately referencing "a little brochure I wrote called A Study in Scarlet," completing the self-referential loop of the series universe.

In terms of its deductive design, however, this work is more mature than A Study in Scarlet. Where that novel divides its structure into "detective solves the puzzle + criminal's memoir," The Sign of the Four embeds its historical backdrop (the Indian Mutiny, the Andaman Islands) entirely within the main investigative thread, so that the very process of finding the killer becomes an act of reading the dark underside of imperial history.

"The Sign of the Four" — the Moral Complexity of the Pact

The moral weight of the novel lies not in the murder itself but in Jonathan Small's "Sign of the Four" oath. He is a criminal whom readers find difficult simply to condemn: during the chaos of the Indian Mutiny he did an unjust thing (conspiracy to rob), yet he was betrayed at a far deeper level by Morstan and Sholto; he served twenty years of hard labour, and at the moment when the Malay treasure was within reach he was robbed yet again.

Doyle allows Small to tell his own story in Chapter 12 — an unusual authorial choice: giving the criminal a first-person account, letting readers feel the logic of his position rather than receiving only the detective's summary. This shift in perspective makes The Sign of the Four a work that inhabits a moral grey zone.

The "Three-Step Lock-in": Doyle's Demonstration of Detective Reasoning

Holmes offers several demonstrations of his method in The Sign of the Four, of which the most representative is his statement of the elimination principle:

"When you have eliminated the impossible, whatever remains, however improbable, must be the truth."

This line appears in Chapter 6 and is the core declaration of Holmes's philosophy. From the standpoint of logic it is an "argument by elimination" — a form of exhaustive disjunctive reasoning — and it is one of the most frequently quoted sentences in the history of detective fiction, and the most enduring legacy Doyle bequeathed to this work.

The Shadow of Imperialism

The Sign of the Four cannot be discussed without addressing its treatment of colonial history — or rather, the way it handles that history is itself worthy of analysis. Tonga is described throughout as "savage," "animal," and "a little devil"; he is the only character in the entire story who never speaks, and who receives no characterisation beyond a name, dying and sinking into the Thames without so much as a moment's acknowledgment.

This stands in stark contrast to the treatment afforded Jonathan Small — who is given a full chapter to tell his own story, and for whom Holmes even speaks up and expresses sympathy. Doyle's imperial worldview is clearly visible in this unequal contrast.
