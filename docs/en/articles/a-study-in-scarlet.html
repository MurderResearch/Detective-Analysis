A Study in Scarlet — Deducibility Analysis

Basic Information

  • Author: Arthur Conan Doyle
  • Year: 1887
  • Type: Novel
  • Detective: Sherlock Holmes
  • Core Mystery Type: whodunit + howdunit + whydunit

Overall Deducibility Rating

Metric Rating Notes
Deducibility ⭐⭐⭐⭐ Average convergence point 78% — Murderer identity: 62%; Method: 75%; Full motive: 90%; Average ≈ 76%, near the four-star threshold
Fog Rating ⭐⭐⭐ A considerable number of red herrings, but the greatest "fog" is structural — the narrative leap in Part Two leaves readers entirely passive, with the author controlling all information release

Synopsis

The story is divided into two distinct parts.

Part One (England, 1881): Retired army doctor John H. Watson meets Holmes in London, and the two become flatmates at 221B Baker Street. On a foggy night, Scotland Yard detective Gregson requests assistance: an American citizen named Enoch Drebber has been found dead in an empty house — no wounds, no robbery, the German word "RACHE" written in blood on the wall, a large quantity of blood on the floor that does not belong to the victim, and a wedding ring at the scene. After collecting clues, Holmes quickly concludes: the murderer is a tall male who arrived by four-wheeled cab, smokes Trichinopoly cigars, and committed the act out of personal grudge rather than politics. Shortly after, the victim's secretary Joseph Stangerson is also murdered at a hotel. Holmes, disguised as a cab driver, identifies the murderer — Jefferson Hope — and has him arrested.

Part Two (Utah, USA, 1847–1860s): The narrative jumps back thirty years, with Doyle using an omniscient perspective to tell the backstory. Explorer John Ferrier and his adopted daughter Lucy join a group of Mormon pioneers heading west, eventually settling and prospering in Utah. Young hunter Jefferson Hope falls in love with Lucy, but Mormon elders use religious threats to force her marriage to Drebber's son. Ferrier is murdered, Lucy is forced to marry and dies of a broken heart. Hope takes the wedding ring from Lucy's dead finger and spends the next twenty years tracking Drebber and Stangerson in pursuit of revenge.

Chapter Six returns to London, where Hope, after his arrest, personally recounts how he committed the murders: he fabricated two identical-looking pills (one poisonous, one harmless) and forced Drebber to choose one while he took the other, gambling with his own life. Drebber drew the poison and died; Hope suffered a nosebleed and used the blood to write "RACHE" on the wall as misdirection. The following day Hope dies in prison from a ruptured aneurysm before he can stand trial.

Truth Two: The Method (howdunit)

  • Convergence Point: approximately 75%
  • Answer: Hope manufactured two identical-looking pills (one poisonous, one harmless), forced Drebber to choose one and swallow it under duress, while he swallowed the other — gambling with his own life. Drebber drew the poison and died; Hope, in his excitement, suffered a nosebleed and used the blood to write "RACHE." In Stangerson's case, when the man refused to cooperate, Hope stabbed him directly.

Reconstructing the Deductive Path

Path One: Identifying the Type of Murderer (whodunit)

A careful reader already has enough physical clues by Part One, Chapter 3 (approx. 19%): the four-wheeled cab tracks, the tall man's stride, and the origin of the blood not being the victim. At this point one should be able to deduce: the murderer is a large, robust male connected to the cab trade, and he has some past connection to the victim (because the victim's expression showed terror — he recognized his killer).

By Chapter 4 (approx. 22%), "Patent-leather [the victim] and Square-toes [the murderer] walked in together in a friendly manner," indicating the murderer did not lie in wait but led the victim inside — a man with a premeditated plan.

By Chapter 5 (approx. 34%), the disguised "old woman" is revealed to be a young, agile male — confirming the murderer is not operating entirely alone and has disguise capability.

By Chapter 7 (approx. 47%), the telegram reply contains the name "Jefferson Hope" — at this point a reader with genuine deductive ability should reasonably suspect this is the murderer. When the Baker Street Irregulars find Hope (approx. 51%), the identity is confirmed.

Path Two: Understanding the Method (howdunit)

"No wounds, large quantities of blood, victim's expression of terror" — these three elements appear simultaneously in Chapter 3 (19%), already forming a powerful mystery. After Holmes sniffs the victim's lips and declares "Poison," a reader who accepts this should begin wondering: what poison? How do you force someone to take poison?

The pill box appears in Chapter 7 (45%), but Lestrade dismisses it as unimportant. This is a major clue left in plain sight. Holmes's pill test (48%) is the final revelation: the "one poisonous, one harmless" pill mechanism, where the murderer designed the victim to choose his own fate — a ritualistic murder with a sense of divine retribution. The full picture of the method converges here.

Path Three: Understanding the Motive (whydunit)

This is the last path to converge. All the hints given in Part One (a woman, American victims, a personal grudge) are only enough to establish the "general direction." Part Two (after 52%) is what finally lets readers see the full picture of events. This design is unusual in detective fiction — the author chooses to force readers to understand the moral justification of the motive before the mystery is solved, but the cost is that readers cannot independently deduce this backstory at all; they can only passively receive it.

Hope's confession (88%) is what finally weaves all three paths together: why the ring was at the scene (because he wanted Drebber to see it as he died), why there was so much blood (a nosebleed, from agitation), and what "RACHE" means (essentially a spontaneous piece of misdirection — Hope himself admits it was a spur-of-the-moment idea).

Additional Notes

This Book's Unique Place in Detective Fiction History

A Study in Scarlet is the first full-length Holmes novel and the first work in which Doyle systematically demonstrates "deduction" as a detective's tool. Yet from the standpoint of mystery design, it is a structurally contradictory founding work.

The Fundamental Structural Problem: The Cost of the "Two-Part" Narrative

This novel consists of two entirely different narratives. Part One (England, present) is a standard detective story in which readers collect clues in tandem with Holmes. Part Two (America, the past) is a Western historical novel that uses omniscient narration to describe events thirty years prior — events that readers in Part One had no way of knowing. This means: all the key information about the motive is forcibly conveyed to readers by the author as "inserted historical background," not deduced by readers themselves.

This is an unusual choice in mystery literature. It makes the "whydunit" dimension almost impossible for readers to deduce in advance — because the clues simply do not exist in the same narrative time and space. Doyle's compensatory mechanism is to make readers feel sympathy for the moral justification of the motive: once they know Hope's story, readers find it hard to see him purely as a criminal who deserved to be caught.

Hope's Moral Ambiguity: The Earliest "Just Avenger" in Detective Fiction History

Jefferson Hope is an extremely rare type of murderer in the history of detective fiction: he does not deny that he killed; he believes himself to be an "executor of justice"; he dies with a peaceful smile on his face. At the story's end, newspapers report that the case "shows foreigners they should settle their grievances in their own countries, not bring them to England" — Doyle thereby deliberately blurs the moral evaluation of the case.

Hope's character foreshadows an important tradition in later detective fiction: the righteous person driven to vengeance. His method of murder (forcing victims to gamble with their lives) also carries a quasi-Old Testament flavor of divine judgment — "let God decide who lives and who dies." The moral complexity of this design makes for an interesting comparison with the obsessive revenge in Poe's The Cask of Amontillado.

Holmes's Methodology as a Showcase

From the standpoint of deducibility, what is interesting in this novel is that Holmes performs many of his deductions and only afterward explains the steps to Watson (and thereby the reader). He announces in Chapter 3 that it is "poisoning," that the murderer is "tall," that the murderer is "connected to a cab" — but a detailed explanation of the methodology doesn't appear until the concluding section of Chapter 7. This design makes it very difficult to "reason alongside Holmes" — what we observe is a deduction already completed, not a process unfolding in real time.

This is in sharp contrast to Agatha Christie's mature-period design. Christie places all clues before the reader, giving readers the opportunity (though a very difficult one) to reason out the answer themselves. Doyle's early design is closer to "a performance by a divine detective" — Holmes is the protagonist, readers are the audience, not fellow competitors on the same reasoning stage.

"RACHE": The Most Successful Red Herring Design

This blood-written word is one of the most cleverly designed elements in A Study in Scarlet. In one stroke it accomplishes three things:

  1. Misleads Lestrade (looking for a woman named Rachel)
  2. Misleads the press and readers (pointing toward a German political secret society)
  3. Is later revealed as the murderer's own improvised act — Hope admits he recalled a German-American murder case in New York and on the spur of the moment decided to use it to confuse the police

The interesting thing about "RACHE" is this: it is both a red herring and a genuine clue. It does indeed point to "revenge" — which is the true motive — but reading it as German leads the investigation astray. Only a reader who thinks about what "revenge" itself truly means, without bias, has any chance of guessing the core of the motive in Part One.

In this work, Doyle simultaneously achieved two goals: "establishing the image of a detective character" and "telling a complete story" — but these two goals have a degree of tension in narrative structure. Detective fiction requires readers to participate in reasoning; storytelling requires the author to control the pace at which information is released. This contradiction is not fully resolved in A Study in Scarlet, but precisely for that reason it has become one of the most fascinating historical specimens in mystery literature.
