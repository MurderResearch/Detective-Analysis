The Adventures of Sherlock Holmes — Deducibility Analysis

Basic Information

  • Author: Arthur Conan Doyle
  • Year: 1892 (individual stories first published in The Strand Magazine, 1891–1892)
  • Type: Short story collection (this analysis covers four representative stories)
  • Detective: Sherlock Holmes
  • Core mystery types: whodunit / howdunit / reverse inference (the detective is defeated)

The collection contains twelve stories in total. This analysis focuses on four chosen for their richest layers of deductive reasoning: 1. A Scandal in Bohemia 2. The Red-Headed League 3. The Adventure of the Blue Carbuncle 4. The Adventure of the Speckled Band

Overall Ratings (Collection-Wide)

Metric Rating Notes
Deducibility ⭐⭐⭐ The average convergence point across the four stories is around 68% — the culprit can usually be identified quite early, but the "how it was done" typically requires until the 70–80% mark to piece together
Fog Rating ⭐⭐⭐ Doyle excels at constructing scenes that appear outwardly impossible to generate fog, reinforced by plausible red herrings, though the degree of deliberate misdirection remains moderate; an attentive reader can outpace Watson in nearly every story

Synopsis (Four Stories)

I. A Scandal in Bohemia

The King of Bohemia secretly visits Holmes and commissions him to recover a photograph taken with opera singer Irene Adler — should it become public, his planned marriage to a Scandinavian princess would be ruined. Holmes disguises himself on several occasions and engineers a "fire alarm" to make Irene instinctively rush toward where the photograph is hidden, believing he has now located it. But the following morning, both Holmes and the King discover that Irene has already fled, leaving behind only a mocking letter: she had long since seen through Holmes's identity and had in turn followed him back to Baker Street to confirm it. This is the only case in the entire collection in which Holmes suffers an outright defeat.

II. The Red-Headed League

Pawnbroker Jabez Wilson is encouraged by his assistant Spaulding to join the "Red-Headed League," a peculiar organization that pays him handsomely to copy out the Encyclopaedia Britannica at their office each morning. After eight weeks, the League abruptly dissolves. Holmes perceives a grand conspiracy beneath the surface: the League's sole purpose was to draw Wilson away from his shop each day so that Spaulding could dig undisturbed in the basement — tunneling toward the vault of a neighboring bank. Spaulding's true identity is John Clay, the most dangerous criminal in London.

III. The Adventure of the Blue Carbuncle

The day after Christmas, Holmes deduces the identity and circumstances of a lost hat's owner, then discovers that a goose contains "the Countess of Morcar's blue carbuncle" — a stolen premier diamond. Holmes traces the goose's origins: from the hat's owner Henry Baker, to the Alpha Inn, to Covent Garden market, and finally catches the real thief red-handed — hotel attendant James Ryder. Ryder confesses to having framed an innocent plumber named Horner using a fake gem, but in transferring the stone he had mixed up the geese, causing the theft to become widely known.

IV. The Adventure of the Speckled Band

Helen Stoner arrives at Baker Street in the middle of the night, alarmed after sleeping in her late sister's bed and hearing the same low whistle her sister reported before her death. Her stepfather, Dr. Grimesby Roylott, is a violent former colonial physician who kept a collection of exotic animals in India and has strong financial motives for preventing his stepdaughters from marrying. Holmes and Watson keep watch in the sealed room through the night and uncover the truth: Roylott had trained an Indian swamp adder to enter the bedroom via a false ventilation shaft and a rope disguised as a bell-pull, then recalled it with a whistle — false wall, false door, false bell-pull, an intricate murder concealed behind the facade of an "impossible crime."

Truth Dissection

Story One: A Scandal in Bohemia

Truth One: Where Is the Photograph Hidden? (howdunit)

  • Convergence point: approximately 65%
  • Answer: Hidden in a recess behind a sliding panel just above the right bell-pull on the wall of the sitting room.
Key Clues
# Clue Position (%) Notes
1 Irene is a trained actress, sharp-minded, and known in the city for her independent life ~15% Establishes that she is capable of concealing sensitive material herself, without relying on others
2 The photograph is cabinet-sized — too large to carry on her person ~30% Narrows the search: it must be in a fixed location within the house
3 Two prior searches of the house and two street interceptions all came up empty ~30% Rules out obvious hiding places and indicates an unconventional method of concealment
4 Holmes perceives the psychological principle that "a woman, in a crisis, will instinctively rush toward whatever she values most" ~55% The core deductive premise: the fire alarm is a technique that works by compelling her to point the way herself
5 When the smoke bomb causes confusion, Irene rushes to the sliding panel on the right-hand wall and briefly pulls it open ~65% Convergence point: the photograph's location is confirmed here

3 Holmes, disguised as a clergyman, is brought into the sitting room to be tended after a staged injury ~58% Allows him to directly observe the sitting room's layout

# Clue Position (%) Misdirection

Truth Two: Irene Had Already Identified and Outmaneuvered Holmes (Reverse Inference)

  • Convergence point: approximately 90%
  • Answer: Irene became suspicious when the smoke bomb was set off, immediately changed into a disguise, followed Holmes to Baker Street to confirm his identity, and fled to the Continent overnight with her husband, leaving a letter explaining her actions.
Key Clues
# Clue Position (%) Notes
1 Irene had been warned in advance: "If the King sends a detective, it will be Sherlock Holmes," and had obtained his address ~90% (revealed in letter) She had preparations for a countermove in place long before the smoke bomb was deployed
2 "Good-night, Mister Sherlock Holmes" — a greeting from the "young man" at the corner of Baker Street ~80% The most important advance warning clue: an attentive reader should sense something is wrong here; but the voice passes so quickly it is easy to miss
3 Irene is accomplished and has worn men's clothing before — it is not unfamiliar to her ~90% (revealed in letter) She has the practical ability to carry out a surveillance operation
4 The next morning, the front door of the house stands open, and the housekeeper waits with a tone of mockery ~88% Everything packed and ready for departure
Red Herrings
# Clue Position (%) Misdirection
1 Holmes's confidence that his plan is foolproof and the photograph can be retrieved the following morning ~70% Leads the reader, following Holmes's perspective, to believe the mission is already accomplished
2 The assumption that Irene's emotional loyalties will shift after marriage ~70% Both Holmes and the King deduce that after her marriage she is no longer a threat — a correct observation in isolation, but one that obscures the warning that "she remains extremely dangerous"

Story Two: The Red-Headed League

Truth One: The Real Purpose of the League (whydunit + howdunit)

  • Convergence point: approximately 68%
  • Answer: To get Wilson out of the pawnshop every morning so that his assistant Spaulding (real name John Clay) could dig undisturbed in the basement, tunneling toward the vault of the City and Suburban Bank behind the shop.
Key Clues
# Clue Position (%) Notes
1 Spaulding accepted employment at half wages — anyone who voluntarily lowers his own price must have an ulterior motive ~15% Holmes is instantly suspicious: no one reduces his own salary without a larger scheme in mind
2 Spaulding is obsessed with "photography" and regularly retreats to the cellar to "develop photographs" ~25% The key metaphor: the basement is the real center of operations; "photography" is a cover
3 The League requires Wilson to remain in the office every morning without leaving for a single moment ~35% Ensures that every morning the pawnshop's owner is absent while the assistant tends the shop alone
4 Holmes taps the pavement with his cane to judge where hollow space lies underground ~63% On-site confirmation that the cellar does not extend forward (not directly in front of Wilson's shop) — it runs toward the rear
5 Holmes deliberately inspects Spaulding's knees — the wear, creasing, and staining ~65% Convergence point: direct physical evidence of prolonged kneeling during excavation
6 The City and Suburban Bank is immediately behind the pawnshop ~68% The final piece: the tunnel's target is confirmed
Supporting Clues
# Clue Position (%) Notes
1 Spaulding has an acid scar on his forehead ~20% For Holmes this confirms the man is John Clay (a known criminal), though a reader may not be able to draw that inference from this clue alone
2 The League's task of "copying the encyclopedia" is absurd and meaningless as work ~30% Reinforces the inference that the whole thing is a ruse, though the reader must still ask: what is the ruse for?
3 The League dissolves on a Saturday ~50% Once the tunnel is complete the bait can be discarded; a Saturday start gives two days of escape time
4 Merryweather is a director of the bank ~75% Confirms that Holmes already knows the target and has made preparations to defend it
Red Herrings
# Clue Position (%) Misdirection
1 The absurd surface appearance of the "Red-Headed League" Throughout first half Leads the reader (and Wilson) to assume the whole affair is a joke or a petty fraud, not the cover for a serious crime
2 The unexplained reason for the League's dissolution ~50% Briefly makes the reader think the criminals have "given up"

Story Three: The Adventure of the Blue Carbuncle

Truth One: Who Is the Real Thief? (whodunit)

  • Convergence point: approximately 70%
  • Answer: The real thief is hotel attendant James Ryder, who conspired with maid Cusack to frame innocent plumber Horner.
Key Clues
# Clue Position (%) Notes
1 The report mentions that "Ryder brought Horner to fix the grate, then left, and returned to find the jewel case had been forced" ~25% Ryder is the only third party who was both present and had opportunity, yet the police do not list him as a suspect
2 Horner has a prior conviction for theft — the ideal conditions for a scapegoat, alerting the reader that someone may have deliberately set a trap ~25% The prior record is a characteristic exploited as a target, not genuine evidence of the crime
3 Henry Baker shows no interest whatsoever in "the remains of the goose" and laughs it off ~55% Confirms that Baker had absolutely no knowledge that the stone was ever inside the goose
4 Breckinridge reacts with unusual irritability and hostility when asked about the provenance of the geese, suggesting someone has been asking repeatedly ~67% Someone else is already tracking the same goose along the same chain
5 The "small, rat-faced" man asking anxious questions at the stall ~70% Convergence point: whoever is actively tracking the stone is the person who placed it there; identity is essentially confirmed
Supporting Clues
# Clue Position (%) Notes
1 The stone was in the goose rather than on Horner's person or at his home ~20% The failure to find it in a search of Horner was not because it was "well hidden" but because it was simply not with him at all
2 The complete tracking chain of the goose: Baker → Alpha Inn → Breckinridge → Mrs. Oakshott (Brixton Road) ~65% The geographical path converges on a person who has a relative on Brixton Road who keeps geese
3 Ryder admits he had been asking at the market about the goose, laying out the entire path of his own undoing ~73% The guilty behavior is what gives him away
Red Herrings
# Clue Position (%) Misdirection
1 Horner is formally charged and breaks down emotionally in court ~22% Leads the reader to think he may actually have done something to feel guilty about
2 The mysterious lost-property owner Henry Baker and the elaborate process of deduction from the hat ~15–50% Holmes's hat analysis passage occupies considerable space and can mislead the reader into thinking "Baker is the key suspect"

Truth Two: How Did the Stone Get Inside the Goose? (howdunit)

  • Convergence point: approximately 85%
  • Answer: Ryder forced the stone down the throat of a goose in Mrs. Oakshott's back yard, but when he returned to collect that goose he got the wrong one — the yard happened to contain two geese with barred tails, he took one, and the stone was inside the other.
Key Clues
# Clue Position (%) Notes
1 Ryder confesses that his sister is married to a man who keeps geese on Brixton Road ~80% Establishes the family connection linking him to the geese
2 Ryder's own account: forced stone into goose → goose broke free and mixed with the flock → took the wrong lookalike goose ~85% Final convergence point: completely explains all the coincidences in the stone's wandering path

Story Four: The Adventure of the Speckled Band

Truth One: The Killer's Identity (whodunit)

  • Convergence point: approximately 25%
  • Answer: Dr. Grimesby Roylott, stepfather to Helen and Julia.
Key Clues
# Clue Position (%) Notes
1 The mother's will: if either daughter marries, she receives £250 per year — enough to place Roylott in serious financial difficulty ~40% The motive is clear: preventing his stepdaughters from marrying is a financial necessity
2 Julia became engaged just two weeks before her death ~20% The engagement triggered the murder plan — this is not a coincidence
3 Roylott travels to Baker Street himself to intimidate Holmes and bends a fireplace poker as a demonstration ~35% An innocent man has no need to threaten a detective; this behavior confirms his fear of the investigation
4 A pretextual repair job causes Helen to move into her dead sister's bedroom, and the timing falls precisely after her own engagement ~30% This is direct evidence of premeditation: the purpose is to get Helen into the same "trap bed"
Supporting Clues
# Clue Position (%) Notes
1 Roylott previously killed a native servant in India in a fit of violence — a record of violent crime ~18% Points to both character and criminal history
2 Roylott keeps exotic Indian animals on the estate and associates closely with gypsies ~22% Establishes that he has both the access and the knowledge to obtain a venomous snake
Red Herrings
# Clue Position (%) Misdirection
1 The gypsies on the estate ~20% Even Holmes initially interprets "speckled band" as the spotted headscarf of the gypsies, leading the reader to suspect vagrant involvement
2 The cheetah and the baboon ~22% The presence of exotic dangerous animals creates an atmosphere of menace and leads the reader to fix on a large mammal as the murder weapon

Truth Two: The Method of Murder (howdunit)

  • Convergence point: approximately 73%
  • Answer: Roylott introduced a trained Indian swamp adder through a ventilation shaft into the bedroom; the snake descended along a rope disguised as a bell-pull to the bed, which was bolted to the floor; the snake was trained with milk as a reward and recalled with a whistle.
Key Clues
# Clue Position (%) Notes
1 Julia could smell the cigar smoke from Roylott's bedroom — some kind of air passage exists between the two rooms ~35% Holmes deduces from this that there must be a ventilation shaft, and that it runs room-to-room rather than to the outside
2 The bell-pull is a dummy — it is not connected to any wire ~65% The turning point: a bell-pull with no function must serve another purpose — as a climbing path for a creeping creature
3 The ventilation shaft is directly above the bell-pull hook and connects only to Roylott's bedroom ~65% The shaft and the rope together form a complete delivery system
4 The bed is bolted to the floor and cannot be moved ~67% Ensures the victim is always directly beneath the end of the rope; this is the physical foundation of the entire mechanism
5 A saucer of milk on top of the iron safe in Roylott's room, though there is no cat in the house ~68% The milk is for the snake — used to train it to obey commands
6 A dog-leash coiled into a loop ~70% A tool for looping around the snake's neck to handle and safely control it
7 Worn marks on the seat of the wooden chair — Roylott habitually stood on it ~72% Convergence point: he stood on the chair to pass the snake through the ventilation shaft into the adjoining room
Supporting Clues
# Clue Position (%) Notes
1 Julia's dying words — "the speckled band" — accompanied by a gesture toward Roylott's room ~20% She described the snake's markings as glimpsed in the light of a match — the most important clue left by the victim
2 The locked-room conditions: door bolted from inside, window barred with iron shutters, chimney with an iron grate — every conventional means of entry is blocked ~22% Establishes that the murder weapon must be something capable of entering and leaving on its own — not a person
3 Julia heard a low whistle on several nights before her death ~20% Roylott was recalling the snake in the night; the sound passed through the ventilation shaft and reached Julia's ears
4 The safe door slightly ajar, with a hidden compartment inside ~68% The snake was ordinarily kept inside the safe; it was released only when the lock was opened
Red Herrings
# Clue Position (%) Misdirection
1 "Speckled band" — a gypsy's spotted headscarf ~20% Even Holmes temporarily pursues the line of thinking that "the gypsies acted as a group"
2 The locked-room mystery (doors and windows secured from within, no means of entry) ~22% Creates the false impression that "no person could have entered, therefore the death was not murder"
3 The cheetah and baboon roaming the estate ~22% Further reinforces the mistaken inference that the weapon was some kind of large animal

Reasoning Path Reconstruction

A Scandal in Bohemia: Two Parallel Paths

Path One (howdunit — where is the photograph)

The reader should notice at around 30% the contradiction: "the photograph must be in the house, yet conventional search methods have failed" — and begin thinking about unconventional hiding places. By 55%, when Holmes puts forward his "fire instinct" theory, the attentive reader should be able to anticipate the plan that follows. The on-site observation at 65% confirms the hiding place.

Path Two (reverse whodunit — who is outmaneuvering whom)

This path is the deeper mystery of the story. The "Good-night, Mister Sherlock Holmes" that appears at 80% is a signal planted for the reader — someone recognized Holmes in the dark and spoke to him directly, meaning someone had followed him. If the reader pauses to think here, they should be able to infer Irene's countermove before reaching the 90% mark.

The Core Reversal of This Story: Holmes wins the tactical engagement (learning where the photograph is hidden) but loses the strategic one (Irene has already moved on). The author leads the reader to believe, through Holmes's perspective, that the plan has been completed — then overturns everything in the final scene. This is not the conventional "reader guesses the truth" game but rather "reader and detective together are outmaneuvered by the sharp-minded subject of the investigation."

The Red-Headed League: An Industrial-Scale Deception

The key question is: "Why would anyone go to such elaborate expense to hire a red-haired fat man?" The reader should notice at around 25% the anomaly that "Spaulding agreed to work at half wages" — this detail is the most important early signal in the entire story. Nobody accepts low pay without reason, unless a larger scheme is at work.

The "basement photography" at 35% is the second signal: the only logical explanation for the basement's appeal is that it leads somewhere. The "knees" at 65% are the confirming clue — a direct visual signal from Doyle to the reader, delivered through Holmes's eyes. Once the neighboring bank is seen at 68%, the complete logical chain of the plan falls neatly into place.

An attentive reader should be able to deduce "tunnel into the bank vault" independently before the 65–68% mark — well ahead of where most readers' instincts would take them.

The Adventure of the Blue Carbuncle: Object-Tracking Reasoning

The reasoning in this story is not "find the culprit" but "track the object." The reader should notice at around 25% the strange gap in the news report: the stone was not found on Horner, yet Ryder — the first person to return to the room — is not under suspicion.

The "someone else also tracking the goose" detail that emerges at 67–70% is the key turning point: an innocent person has no reason to be frantically inquiring about a goose that has already been sold; only the person who concealed the stone inside it would be in such urgency. By the time Ryder's identity is revealed (around 73%), the entire chain is sharply in focus.

The finest touch in this story is that the complete revelation of the truth (the stone ended up in the wrong goose) is itself a comic "accident" — the tension of a near-murder dissolves instantly into farce, and the story closes with Holmes's decision to let Ryder go free: ethically debatable, but humanly impeccable.

The Adventure of the Speckled Band: Decoding the Locked Room in Reverse

The cleverest aspect of this story is that the reader can identify the killer very early — around 25% — but the heart of the mystery is "how it was done." This transforms the whodunit into a pure howdunit puzzle.

The reasoning path proceeds as follows:

  1. ~20%: "Speckled band" and "finger pointing toward the stepfather's room" → Julia was trying to accuse someone, but the description is ambiguous
  2. ~35%: "Cigar smoke that could penetrate the wall" → there must be an air passage, and it runs room-to-room
  3. ~65%: "False bell-pull + ventilation shaft on the same axis" → the rope is a passageway, for some kind of creeping creature to use
  4. ~68%: "Saucer of milk on the safe, but no cat" → there is an animal in the safe; the milk is for taming
  5. ~72%: "Worn chair seat + dog-leash loop" → Roylott stood on the chair to release the snake, and used the leash to retrieve it

By 72%, the reader should be able to independently construct the complete mechanism — "a trained snake sent through the ventilation shaft, descending the rope onto the bolted-down bed to kill" — before Holmes states the answer aloud.

Supplementary Notes

Doyle's Philosophy of Deductive Design

The Adventures of Sherlock Holmes represents Arthur Conan Doyle's detective writing at its mature peak. Compared to the novels A Study in Scarlet and The Sign of the Four, these short stories are structurally more refined, their clues more concentrated, and their "tricks" more accomplished as literature. Doyle established a template for "displayed reasoning" that remains widely employed today:

  1. The opening impromptu deduction (reading the client): almost every story opens with Holmes's "reading" of the visitor or a physical object — simultaneously building intellectual anticipation and establishing the habits of observation that will prove necessary for solving the case
  2. Inverted information asymmetry: the reader and Watson are synchronized with each other, and both lag behind Holmes. Doyle deliberately allows Holmes to "already know but not yet explain," creating a gentle narrative tension
  3. Objects as mediators: nearly every case has a central object (hat, photograph, goose, rope); the reasoning begins with the object, traces the object, and concludes with the object

Comparison and Positioning of the Four Stories

Story Deducibility Fog Rating Core Innovation
A Scandal in Bohemia ⭐⭐⭐⭐ ⭐⭐⭐ The detective is defeated; intellectual countermove by the subject of the investigation
The Red-Headed League ⭐⭐⭐ ⭐⭐⭐ An elaborate swindle as a criminal instrument; "absurd premise, rigorous logic"
The Adventure of the Blue Carbuncle ⭐⭐⭐⭐ ⭐⭐⭐ Object-tracking logic; a comically accidental crime; a morally ambiguous act of clemency
The Adventure of the Speckled Band ⭐⭐ ⭐⭐⭐⭐ Locked-room device; a snake as murder weapon; the deconstruction of "the impossible"

The Literary Standing of "A Scandal in Bohemia"

"The woman" — Irene Adler's status as the most celebrated supporting character in the history of detective fiction stems precisely from the fact that she is the only person to defeat Holmes comprehensively at the intellectual level. Doyle uses Watson's admiring narration to accustom the reader to the expectation that "Holmes always wins" — and then shatters that expectation in the very first story. This opening gambit is extremely shrewd: it establishes Holmes as a figure of immense power who is nonetheless not invincible.

The Deductive Flaw in "The Adventure of the Speckled Band"

This story is a famous point of controversy in the history of detective fiction. The problem is this: no snake exists in reality that can be trained to respond to a whistle, crawl through a ventilation shaft in darkness, and descend a rope to a precise location on a bed — let alone one that drinks milk (snakes do not lap milk) or responds to whistling as an auditory stimulus (snakes are nearly deaf and perceive their environment primarily through vibration). The solution is intellectually compelling but zoologically complete fiction. Doyle himself later acknowledged this as an error in his knowledge, though it does not prevent the story from being one of the most celebrated locked-room murders in the history of detective fiction — its pacing, its atmosphere of dread, and the snake that finally turns on its master make it, in terms of literary effect, without fault.

Contribution to the Detective Tradition

These short stories established the modern form of the detective short story: a fixed detective, a fixed companion (who also serves as narrator), cases brought by clients, the reasoning process presented transparently (though with a slight delay), and a methodological explanation appended after the truth is revealed. This structure directly influenced Agatha Christie (whose Poirot series is in structure almost a refined elaboration of this template) and became the skeleton of the twentieth-century detective novel.
