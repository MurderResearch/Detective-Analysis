# Arsène Lupin, Gentleman-Burglar — Deducibility Analysis

## Basic Information
- Author: Maurice Leblanc
- Published: 1907 (stories serialized individually 1905–1907, collected in book form)
- Type: Short story collection (9 stories)
- Detective: No traditional detective — Arsène Lupin himself serves simultaneously as criminal, detective, and the mystery's core
- Core mystery type: identity mystery / howdunit / whodunit

---

## Synopsis

*Arsène Lupin, Gentleman-Burglar* is Leblanc's collection of serialized short stories written for the French magazine *Je sais tout*, and it is also the first book in the Arsène Lupin series. Nine stories revolve around the same central figure — Arsène Lupin — yet each adopts a different mystery structure and narrative strategy.

Arsène Lupin is not a detective; he is a criminal genius. But because Leblanc makes him smarter than every detective in the stories, readers are compelled to understand the mystery from his point of view. This gives rise to one of the strangest paradoxes in the history of detective fiction: **the answer to the mystery is very often Arsène Lupin himself** — his identity, his plan, his method.

Compared with Conan Doyle's Holmes, the challenge of Lupin's mysteries comes from an entirely different dimension: the reader is not "finding the criminal" but "keeping up with Arsène Lupin's thinking" — and Leblanc spends enormous effort ensuring the reader cannot.

## In-Depth Analysis: Four Selected Stories

### Story I: "The Arrest of Arsène Lupin"

#### Basic Information
- Core mystery: The true identity of narrator "Monsieur d'Andrézy"
- Type: identity mystery + howdunit

#### Deducibility Rating

| Metric | Rating | Notes |
|--------|--------|-------|
| Deducibility | ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ | Convergence point ~90% — the truly decisive clue (the Kodak camera) does not fully surface until the moment before arrest |
| Fog Rating | ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ | The narrator is the criminal; the entire story is told from the perspective of an "innocent bystander," systematically deceiving the reader |

#### Story Summary

Aboard the transatlantic ocean liner *La Provence*, a telegram alerts the entire ship: a blond male passenger in first class whose name begins with R is Arsène Lupin. The narrator, "Monsieur d'Andrézy," uses wit and deduction to help narrow the list of suspects, ultimately pointing toward Rozaine, who is likewise blond and traveling alone. Rozaine is briefly arrested but released when no scar is found, and the situation descends into chaos. When the ship arrives in New York harbor, detective Ganimard stops not Rozaine but the narrator himself — he is the true Arsène Lupin.

#### Truth Dissection

**Truth 1: Narrator d'Andrézy is Arsène Lupin**
- **Clue convergence point: ~90%**
- Answer: The narrator of the entire story is not an innocent bystander but Arsène Lupin himself. He deliberately directs the initial-R misdirection toward Rozaine while actively "assisting" the investigation to ensure he remains above suspicion.

##### Key Clues

| # | Clue | Position (%) | Notes |
|---|------|-------------|-------|
| 1 | When Rozaine is asked to show his arm, he **shows the left arm** rather than the right | ~25% | The telegram clearly stated the scar was on the "right forearm." A person genuinely trying to prove their innocence would not show the wrong arm — yet at that moment nearly no reader notices this detail |
| 2 | The narrator has an abnormally accurate "intuition" about Arsène Lupin's psychology and methods, as though describing himself | ~40–60% | His prediction that Arsène Lupin "would not permit anyone to search his luggage" far exceeds the knowledge any ordinary passenger should possess |
| 3 | The narrator proactively offers to lend his Kodak camera to Nelly for photographs, and just before his arrest deliberately "places the camera in her hands" | ~85–90% | This is the single most critical action — he transfers the camera at the last instant before arrest because stolen goods are hidden inside. This action can only be recognized as meaningful by looking back after the truth is known |
| 4 | "What a pity that I am not an honest man!" — a murmur to himself after being arrested | ~95% | A last-moment confession, and the only utterance that unambiguously points to the narrator's true identity |

##### Supporting Clues

| # | Clue | Position (%) | Notes |
|---|------|-------------|-------|
| 1 | Nelly initially fears the "silent young man" (Rozaine) who enters her compartment — suggesting that her later ease around d'Andrézy means she has been fooled | ~15% | Readers should note that Nelly's instincts are often more reliable than her conscious reasoning |
| 2 | The narrator actively intervenes in the investigation and takes the initiative in narrowing the list of suspects — a suspicious motive for an "innocent bystander" | Throughout | Truly innocent people do not typically assist an investigation with such enthusiasm |
| 3 | The name d'Andrézy belongs to a real person, but that person died in Macedonia three years ago | ~95% (revealed by Ganimard) | Disclosed only at the moment of arrest; a retrospective clue |

##### Red Herrings

| # | Clue | Position (%) | Misdirection |
|---|------|-------------|-------------|
| 1 | Rozaine's name begins with R, he is blond, and he is traveling alone — perfectly matching the telegram's description | ~20–30% | Focuses readers and passengers alike on suspecting Rozaine, distracting from the fact that the telegram itself is a false lead planted by Arsène Lupin |
| 2 | Rozaine is genuinely attacked and robbed, and a card accepting the bounty is found on him | ~45% | Leads readers to believe Rozaine is truly a victim, ruling out the possibility that he is Arsène Lupin — while the attack was orchestrated by Arsène Lupin himself |
| 3 | The telegram itself: "a blond passenger whose name begins with R" | ~5% | The telegram itself is a decoy pre-arranged by Arsène Lupin to draw everyone's attention toward Rozaine |

#### Reasoning Path Reconstruction

**The reasoning path for the identity mystery**

At around 25%, a careful reader ought to stop and ask: "Why did Rozaine show the left arm instead of the right?" This is the only "immediately perceptible contradiction" in the entire story, yet it is so small, so buried under Ganimard's actions, that the vast majority of readers will skip right over it.

If the reader does retain this detail and then reads, between 40% and 60%, about the narrator's "abnormal familiarity" with Arsène Lupin's psychology, suspicion should begin to form. But Leblanc deliberately fills the narrator's voice with upper-class refinement and ease, making it difficult to associate him with a criminal.

The truly decisive moment arrives at 85%: the narrator proactively hands the Kodak to Nelly. At that moment the action appears to be "letting Nelly take photographs," yet looking back after knowing the truth, one understands this as the transfer of stolen goods. This clue is visible only after the answer is known — and that is Leblanc's most masterly technique.

**Leblanc's narrative trick**

This story deploys the most dangerous weapon in detective fiction: **a first-person unreliable narrator who is himself the criminal.** The reader stands on Arsène Lupin's shoulders "watching" the story throughout, yet never realizes that the viewpoint has been engineered from the start. This technique was a stunning avant-garde achievement in 1905 — Poe's "The Cask of Amontillado" has a similar design, but there the reader knows from the outset that the narrator is the killer; Leblanc's version is complete deception.

### Story II: "The Escape of Arsène Lupin"

#### Basic Information
- Core mystery: How does Arsène Lupin vanish from a tightly guarded prison, and who is "Baudru Désiré"?
- Type: howdunit (method mystery) + identity mystery

#### Deducibility Rating

| Metric | Rating | Notes |
|--------|--------|-------|
| Deducibility | ⭐⭐⭐⭐ | Convergence point ~80% — not confirmed until Arsène Lupin reveals "Baudru is him," but foreshadowing clues are scattered throughout |
| Fog Rating | ⭐⭐⭐⭐ | Multiple layers of false escape plans (modified prison van, secret letters) constitute carefully engineered double misdirection |

#### Story Summary

From prison, Arsène Lupin publicly announces "I will not attend my own trial." Police intercept a secret letter and discover an escape plan, deploying heavy security. On the day of the trial, a ragged vagrant named "Baudru Désiré" appears in the dock — nothing like Arsène Lupin. Ganimard swears in open court that "this man is not Arsène Lupin," causing an uproar. The trial is suspended; Arsène Lupin has vanished. After his release, Ganimard follows Baudru through the woods — and in a moment of solitude hears that laugh he can never forget. It is Arsène Lupin.

#### Truth Dissection

**Truth 1: Baudru Désiré is Arsène Lupin in disguise**
- **Clue convergence point: ~80%**
- Answer: During the two months following his transfer to a new cell, Arsène Lupin used chemical agents, constant practice, and starvation to gradually transform his appearance into that of Baudru — a real vagrant who bore some resemblance to him. At trial, he knew that if anyone "examined him with the assumption that he might be Arsène Lupin" he would be recognized; therefore his most important line of defense was to get Ganimard to volunteer the declaration "this man is not Arsène Lupin."

##### Key Clues

| # | Clue | Position (%) | Notes |
|---|------|-------------|-------|
| 1 | After being transferred to the new cell, Arsène Lupin spends two consecutive months "lying quietly with his face to the wall," leading the guards to believe he has fallen into despair | ~40% | This "silent period" is the time for his systematic physical transformation, but the text packages it as "depression following a setback" |
| 2 | At trial, the defendant's appearance is "pale, gaunt, hollow-cheeked — utterly unlike the photographs in the newspapers" | ~70% | The extreme physical change ought to arouse suspicion, yet it is interpreted as "the ravages of prison life" |
| 3 | In the courtroom Ganimard feels "a strange unease," studies the defendant repeatedly, and ultimately approaches for a closer look | ~72% | Ganimard's instinct briefly surfaces here, but is suppressed by the presiding judge's forceful words |
| 4 | After his release, Baudru emits "that hellishly familiar laugh" on a bench in the Bois de Boulogne | ~90% | This is the final confirmation — the laugh is an identifier impossible to counterfeit |

##### Supporting Clues

| # | Clue | Position (%) | Notes |
|---|------|-------------|-------|
| 1 | Arsène Lupin says from prison: "My measurements do not exist — or rather, they are false" | ~80% (explained by Arsène Lupin afterward) | He had bribed Bertillon measurement bureau personnel in America before his arrest |
| 2 | Arsène Lupin had studied skin diseases under Dr. Altier at the Saint-Louis Hospital — a detail in his background mentioned by the judge during the Ch. III proceedings | ~68% | This history is presented as an "odd aspect of Arsène Lupin's past," but it is actually the source of his disguise technique |

##### Red Herrings

| # | Clue | Position (%) | Misdirection |
|---|------|-------------|-------------|
| 1 | A secret letter concealed in a cigar describes a complex "escape plan" (modified prison van, getaway car) | ~50–55% | Draws the attention of police (and readers) to that "failed escape attempt," causing them to miss the real plan — identity substitution — already in progress |
| 2 | The "failed escape attempt" (jumping from the prison van, appearing publicly in a café, then voluntarily returning to prison) | ~60% | Arsène Lupin publicizes this event deliberately to establish the public expectation "Arsène Lupin will certainly try to escape," priming everyone in the courtroom to accept the notion "that person is a stand-in" |

#### Reasoning Path Reconstruction

The most brilliant aspect of this story is that Arsène Lupin turns **public expectation itself** into a criminal instrument.

At around 40%, a careful reader should note that "the two-month silent period" is unusual. Arsène Lupin has never been a quiet man; this behavioral change requires explanation. But Leblanc provides a seemingly adequate reason — depression following the cell transfer — and readers accept it.

At 70%, the description of the defendant's appearance at trial is extremely abnormal: vacant eyes, lopsided posture, no response to speech. This is not merely "exhaustion from prison life" — it looks more like a deliberately performed state of mental incapacity. A careful reader should begin to feel suspicious here.

The true confirmation point arrives at 90%: Arsène Lupin's laugh. This is a clue that cannot be rationally "deduced," because it depends on Ganimard's physical memory rather than the reader's cognitive reasoning. This is Leblanc's deliberate design — the mystery of Arsène Lupin's identity can ultimately be unlocked only by "perception," not by "logic."

### Story III: "The Queen's Necklace"

#### Basic Information
- Core mystery: Who stole the carefully guarded queen's necklace, and how was it done?
- Type: howdunit (locked-room mystery) + whodunit + identity mystery

#### Deducibility Rating

| Metric | Rating | Notes |
|--------|--------|-------|
| Deducibility | ⭐⭐⭐⭐ | Average convergence point ~77% — the method can be deduced at ~70%, but the thief's identity cannot be confirmed until ~85% |
| Fog Rating | ⭐⭐⭐ | The principal misdirection focuses on making readers suspect Henriette (the mother) rather than her young son |

#### Story Summary

Count and Countess Dreux-Soubise own a historic diamond necklace of the queen, which is locked away in a small inner chamber adjoining the bedroom each time it is worn. One morning it disappears: the chamber window is locked, the bedroom door bolted from within — a perfect locked room. The investigation turns to their close friend Henriette and her young son Raoul, who lived in the same building. Years later the "Chevalier Floriani" pays a visit and effortlessly "reasons out" the solution — the thief was six-year-old Raoul, Henriette's son; as an adult he has returned to the scene of the crime under the alias "Floriani," and he is none other than the future Arsène Lupin.

#### Truth Dissection

**Truth 1: The thief is six-year-old Raoul — the future Arsène Lupin**
- **Clue convergence point: ~85%**
- Answer: Raoul used a wooden plank from the kitchen as a bridge across the small courtyard, hooked open the ventilation slit at the top of the chamber window with a bent fire poker, and crawled inside to take the necklace. He sold the diamonds one by one at school to support his gravely ill mother.

##### Key Clues

| # | Clue | Position (%) | Notes |
|---|------|-------------|-------|
| 1 | Henriette's kitchen window "faces directly onto the chamber window," the two windows "at least three meters apart" | ~45% | Floriani later points out that three meters is an obstacle for an adult but can be bridged by a child with a plank |
| 2 | The child Raoul "had just come back from school and was sitting reading" on the morning of the theft | ~50% | This detail is mentioned deliberately, but readers assume the child is an innocent bystander; in fact "had just come back from school" means he had just been active outside |
| 3 | Over six years, Henriette receives two thousand francs annually in anonymous remittances, sent from different cities under different signatures | ~55–65% | Anonymous remittances imply the sender wishes to conceal their identity — "a guilt-ridden insider" is logically more plausible than "an inexplicably charitable stranger." The question is: who would know Henriette's address? Who cares about her situation? |
| 4 | Floriani asks about the chamber's "ventilation slit" and accurately deduces its location and the way it opens | ~72% | His excessive accuracy in "reasoning" suggests he is not deducing at all — he is remembering |
| 5 | As Floriani's account progresses it increasingly resembles first-hand experience; he uses phrasing like "what he imagined Henriette's son must feel upon seeing this family again" | ~85% | At this point a careful reader should have confirmed: Floriani is Raoul; Floriani is that child |

##### Supporting Clues

| # | Clue | Position (%) | Notes |
|---|------|-------------|-------|
| 1 | Investigation finds no plausible entry or exit point — "a person cannot simply vanish" — yet a child's body can pass through spaces an adult cannot | ~35–50% | Investigators consistently assume the crime was committed by an adult and therefore overlook the possibility of a child |
| 2 | After being dismissed and rebuked by the Countess, Henriette shows no anger — only silent acceptance — like a person who knows "the truth cannot be told" | ~60% | Her silence is not guilt but protection of her son |
| 3 | The Count finally confirms: the kitchen wooden plank "had been removed," and the fire poker "was still there" | ~82% | The physical evidence matches Floriani's reasoning |

##### Red Herrings

| # | Clue | Position (%) | Misdirection |
|---|------|-------------|-------------|
| 1 | Henriette "knew the necklace was kept in the chamber at night" | ~45% | Leads readers and investigators to concentrate suspicion on Henriette rather than her son |
| 2 | Investigators find the window "locked from the inside, completely sealed" | ~35% | Leads to the conclusion that entry was impossible, while the ventilation slit — a path requiring some architectural knowledge to conceive — is overlooked |
| 3 | After the trial, the judge leans toward the view "the Count and Countess sold the necklace to raise money" | ~65% | Leads readers to think the mystery may have no real answer — merely an insurance fraud |

#### Reasoning Path Reconstruction

**The howdunit reasoning path**

At 45%, the position of Henriette's kitchen already forms the foundation for deducing the method. If readers remember "the kitchen window faces directly onto the chamber window" and then apply Floriani's logic of "there must be a way to bridge the gap," they can independently work out the method at ~70%. The howdunit difficulty of this mystery is moderate.

**The whodunit reasoning path**

The difficulty is that most readers will not think of "a six-year-old child." Cultural assumption tells us that a precise, meticulous theft is adult behavior. Leblanc understands this, and so at 50% he buries the hint that Raoul "had just come back from school," presenting it as an innocuous detail.

The clue that truly allows readers to "confirm Raoul" is the excessively personal emotion in Floriani's words at 85% — "he imagined how that child must feel upon seeing this family again" — a kind of narration only someone who had lived it would produce. At ~90%, the Count directly asks "Who are you?"; Floriani's smile without answer completes the confirmation.

### Story IV: "The Black Pearl"

#### Basic Information
- Core mystery: Who murdered the Countess? Where is the Black Pearl hidden?
- Type: whodunit + location-of-object mystery

#### Deducibility Rating

| Metric | Rating | Notes |
|--------|--------|-------|
| Deducibility | ⭐⭐⭐ | Average convergence point ~65% — sufficient clues for the killer's identity exist at ~65%, but the pearl's hiding place cannot be deduced until ~85% |
| Fog Rating | ⭐⭐⭐ | The principal misdirection comes from the identity of Arsène Lupin as the "3 a.m. visitor" and from Danègre's acquittal |

#### Story Summary

The celebrated actress Leotine Zalti, Countess d'Andillot, is stabbed to death in her home; the heirloom black pearl she always kept with her goes missing. Police arrest manservant Victor Danègre: a bloodstained dress coat is found in his room, and a button that fell from that coat is found at the scene. But the defense attorney points out that no one saw the key, no one saw the murder weapon, and the time of the crime (11:20 p.m.) contradicts the hour a mysterious visitor entered the premises (3 a.m.) — Danègre is ultimately acquitted. After his release, Arsène Lupin, disguised as a former detective, threatens him with the damning physical evidence he possesses and forces him to reveal where the Black Pearl is hidden.

#### Truth Dissection

**Truth 1: Victor Danègre is the murderer**
- **Clue convergence point: ~65%**
- Answer: Three weeks earlier, Danègre had duplicated the spare key to the kitchen door, premeditated the crime. Using his knowledge of the Countess's habit of hiding the Black Pearl inside a stationery box, he entered her bedroom while she slept — was discovered by her — and killed her.

##### Key Clues

| # | Clue | Position (%) | Notes |
|---|------|-------------|-------|
| 1 | Danègre's bloodstained dress coat is missing a button, and that button was found at the crime scene | ~55% | The physical evidence is solid; but the defense argues it was "planted by someone else" — Arsène Lupin later reveals the exact opposite is true: Danègre himself left the evidence |
| 2 | The stopped clock reads 11:20 p.m. (the time of the crime), and the Countess "was in the habit of retiring early" — at 11:20 p.m. she would already have been asleep | ~55% | The time of the crime falls within the plausible range of her daily routine and fits someone who knew her — an inside actor |
| 3 | Danègre left the apartment only once each day: to the tobacconist around the corner — this outing occurred in the early morning after the crime | ~60% | His range of movement is extremely limited; the pearl must have been hidden somewhere along his route |
| 4 | The "mysterious visitor" at 3 a.m. (i.e., Arsène Lupin) was **not** the murderer — when he arrived the Countess was already dead and the clock already showed 11:20 p.m. | ~60% | The gap between the two times (3 a.m. and 11:20 p.m.) actually confirms: the crime occurred at 11:20 p.m., and the murderer entered and departed before 3 a.m. |

##### Supporting Clues

| # | Clue | Position (%) | Notes |
|---|------|-------------|-------|
| 1 | Danègre "has a bad background — a known drunkard and gambler" | ~55% | Suggests motive: financially driven, with a prior criminal record |
| 2 | The Countess's cousin, Mlle. de Sinclèves, received a letter describing how the Black Pearl was hidden — the letter subsequently vanished | ~55% | The disappeared letter indicates that someone knew in advance where the pearl was hidden and actively eliminated evidence that might expose them |
| 3 | Arsène Lupin (the 3 a.m. visitor) took away the murder weapon and the spare key — items that later become the "damning evidence" used to threaten Danègre | ~90% (revealed retrospectively) | Arsène Lupin was already certain at that moment that Danègre was the killer, and so he preserved the evidence |

##### Red Herrings

| # | Clue | Position (%) | Misdirection |
|---|------|-------------|-------------|
| 1 | The mystery of "the 3 a.m. mysterious visitor" | ~50–70% | Draws the investigative focus of readers and police toward "who that person was" rather than "who was present at 11:20 p.m." |
| 2 | Danègre is "acquitted" by jury verdict | ~70% | A legal acquittal leads readers to abandon suspicion of him — but legal innocence is not the same as factual innocence |
| 3 | The defense attorney's argument: "without a key, he could not have locked up on his way out" | ~68% | This "flaw" is actually illusory — he did have a spare key; it was simply taken away by Arsène Lupin |

**Truth 2: The Black Pearl is hidden in a crack between paving stones on the sidewalk**
- **Clue convergence point: ~85%**
- Answer: Danègre's single outing (to the tobacconist early in the morning) was his only opportunity to conceal the pearl. The crack between the paving stones near the tobacconist was the only place where he could quickly and inconspicuously hide an object.

##### Key Clues (Pearl's Hiding Place)

| # | Clue | Position (%) | Notes |
|---|------|-------------|-------|
| 1 | Before being arrested, Danègre "had left the apartment only once" — to go to the tobacconist | ~60% | Both a clue and a geographic constraint: the pearl must be somewhere near that route |
| 2 | The tobacconist is "a few steps away" from the Countess's home — the same city block | ~60% | Narrows the search radius |
| 3 | Danègre indicates "the crack between the twelfth and thirteenth paving stones counting from the right of the drain" | ~92% (forced confession) | Made explicit only at this point, but logically inferable at ~85% |

#### Reasoning Path Reconstruction

This story is logically the closest to a traditional mystery puzzle among the nine in the collection, yet Leblanc raises the difficulty with two techniques.

**First technique: the smoke screen of the mysterious visitor**

The "mysterious visitor" at 3 a.m. (actually Arsène Lupin) creates a perfect smoke screen: readers begin to wonder "perhaps it was that visitor who committed the murder." This suspicion occupies too much attention between 50% and 70%, obscuring the true window of the crime established by the clock (11:20 p.m.). If readers can realize at 60% that "the two times differ by nearly four hours and describe two different people," they can shift their focus back to the 11:20 p.m. scene.

**Second technique: the psychological effect of legal acquittal**

Danègre's acquittal inclines readers (and readers' instinctive sense of justice) toward "if he was acquitted, then he truly is innocent." Leblanc challenges the reader's understanding here: the outcome of a legal proceeding is not identical to factual truth. Arsène Lupin knew the truth from the beginning; his plan was to let the legal system first "convict, then acquit" and then strike when Danègre's psychological defenses were at their weakest.

## Overall Reasoning Path Reconstruction (Collection Perspective)

The nine stories of *Arsène Lupin, Gentleman-Burglar* share a deep common structure: **the core of the mystery is almost always "who is Arsène Lupin" or "what is Arsène Lupin doing."** This is entirely different from the traditional mystery novel's "who is the murderer."

When reading this collection, the reader must abandon two standard deductive habits:

1. **Do not trust the narrator** — in many stories, the first-person narrator or the character who "feels most like the protagonist" is very often Arsène Lupin himself.
2. **Do not trust official investigative conclusions** — court acquittals, the formal results of police searches: in Arsène Lupin's world these tend to be precisely the opposite of the truth.

The true "deductive reader" should ask themselves at the start of each story: "In this scene, whose behavior is the most fluid, the most strategic? That is Arsène Lupin."

## Additional Notes

**Leblanc's narrative revolution**

Between 1905 and 1907, Conan Doyle's Holmes series was at its peak. Leblanc created Arsène Lupin within that context and accomplished something that seemed impossible: **he made readers love a criminal they could never see through.**

The structural logic of Holmes's mysteries is "readers solve the puzzle alongside the detective"; the structural logic of Arsène Lupin's mysteries is "readers commit the crime alongside the criminal — but always one step behind." Both patterns established separate traditions in the history of detective fiction. Wilkie Collins's early anti-hero criminal stories, and the later "inverted mystery" form (in which the crime method is narrated first and then solved by a detective), can all find their antecedents in the Arsène Lupin series.

**The distinctive nature of the deducibility**

Unlike Christie's readers, who can engage in "fair play puzzle-solving," the difficulty of Arsène Lupin's mysteries derives largely from **an inherent asymmetry in the narrative structure itself.** What the reader lacks is not intelligence but information — because Arsène Lupin always knows more than the narrator (including when he is his own narrator).

This gives the Deducibility ratings in this collection a special quality: even if every clue is annotated and flagged, those clues can only be recognized as "clues" after the answer is already known. This is Leblanc's most thorough design — he constructs a labyrinth whose entrance map can only be read from the exit.

**Comparison with Doyle's Holmes**

Leblanc does not conceal his homage to (and provocation of) Doyle — in the ninth story Sherlock Holmes appears in person, yet Arsène Lupin wins their encounter with impeccable courtesy. This story is deliberately made too transparent at the mystery level (Sherlock Holmes solves the hidden-passage riddle in ten minutes), yet it is thought-provoking at the human level: in an age of competing intellects, when the two cleverest men meet, they each choose to walk away.

**A note on historical context**

"The Queen's Necklace" alludes to a real historical scandal on the eve of the French Revolution (the "Affair of the Diamond Necklace," 1785). Historically, Cardinal de Rohan and the impostor Jeanne de la Motte used forged letters in the queen's name to defraud a jeweler, triggering a scandal that gravely damaged Marie Antoinette's reputation and indirectly accelerated the coming revolution. Leblanc uses this genuine historical episode as a backdrop and lets the fictional Arsène Lupin become the solver of a historical mystery — which is itself a carefully designed "meta-mystery."
