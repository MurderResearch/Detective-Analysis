# The Hound of the Baskervilles — Deducibility Analysis

## Basic Information
- Author: Arthur Conan Doyle
- Year: 1902 (serialised in The Strand Magazine, 1901–1902)
- Type: Novel
- Detective: Sherlock Holmes
- Core mystery type: whodunit + howdunit + whydunit

## Synopsis

On the moors of Devonshire, the Baskerville family has carried a dread curse through the generations: the ancestral Hugo Baskerville brought upon himself a hellish hound through his wickedness, and ever since, any descendant who transgresses the forbidden moor is said to die with the beast's teeth at his throat.

Sir Charles Baskerville is found dead one night on the Yew Alley of the estate — his face twisted in horror, his body bearing no wounds, yet his footprints revealing that in his final moments he had fled in wild panic. His family physician, Dr. Mortimer, discovers the enormous pawprint of a hound near the corpse, but conceals this fact from the inquest out of fear of causing panic. He travels to London to seek Holmes's help, arriving at the same time as Sir Charles's heir — the young Sir Henry Baskerville, returning from Canada to claim the estate.

Sir Henry has barely reached London before strange events begin to accumulate: a threatening letter assembled from newspaper clippings, a brand-new boot that vanishes from his hotel room, and a mysterious black-bearded man who shadows his every move. Holmes dispatches Watson to accompany Sir Henry to Baskerville Hall, while he himself, under the pretence of remaining in London on other business, secretly takes up residence in an ancient stone hut on the moor to conduct his own investigation.

On the moor, suspects multiply: the loyal yet secretive butler Barrymore and his wife signal with candlelight in the dead of night; the naturalist Stapleton — learned, cool-headed, and intimately familiar with every inch of the moor — reacts with alarming fury when Sir Henry begins to court his supposed "sister" Beryl; the notorious murderer Selden prowls the wasteland; and the eccentric, well-to-do Frankland keeps watch over the moor day and night through a powerful telescope.

As the investigation deepens, the truth is peeled back layer by layer: Stapleton is not the ordinary naturalist he claims to be, and Beryl is not his sister. He has kept a great hound — coated in phosphorus so that it blazes like a will-o'-the-wisp in the darkness — hidden in the depths of Grimpen Mire, training it to recognise Sir Henry's scent using a stolen boot, then used Laura Lyons to lure Sir Charles to the gate of the Yew Alley late at night before releasing the beast. Sir Charles, a man with a weak heart, was frightened to death in his flight. Stapleton then set his sights on Sir Henry. Holmes springs a trap at the last moment and shoots the hound dead with three bullets. Stapleton flees in panic into Grimpen Mire and is never seen again, almost certainly swallowed by the bog.

## Truth Analysis

### Truth One: The Killer's True Identity (whodunit)

- **Clue convergence point: approximately 82%**
- Answer: Stapleton's true identity is Rodger Baskerville — the illegitimate son of Sir Charles's younger brother Roger, and therefore the hidden heir to the Baskerville fortune. He assumed the name Stapleton, brought his wife Beryl to the moor disguised as his sister, and spent two years lying in wait with the aim of eliminating every heir who stood before him so that he could claim a fortune of nearly one million pounds.

#### Key Clues

| # | Clue | Location (chapter / approx. %) | Notes |
|---|------|-------------------------------|-------|
| 1 | Stapleton claims to have run a school in the north of England that failed due to an epidemic, after which he moved to the moor — arriving shortly after Sir Charles returned to the Hall | Ch. 7 / ~37% | The timing of his arrival corresponds closely with Sir Charles's return, suggesting his presence was deliberate and planned; the school story is later proved to be a lie |
| 2 | Roger Baskerville (Sir Charles's younger brother) is said to have died of yellow fever in 1876, but the account is vague and no one actually confirmed it | Ch. 4 / ~28% | This leaves an unfilled gap in the family line of inheritance — the very gap from which Stapleton emerges |
| 3 | When Holmes investigates Stapleton's claim to have been a schoolmaster, he traces records of a school run under the name "Vandeleur" and confirms that Stapleton previously operated under a different name before disappearing with his wife | Ch. 12 / ~78% | This is the **first decisive clue** confirming that Stapleton fabricated his identity |
| 4 | An old portrait in the Hall — Holmes covers the hat and long hair in the painting, and "Stapleton's face" leaps out from a seventeenth-century canvas | Ch. 13 / ~82% | The **final key clue**: Stapleton is a blood descendant of old Hugo Baskerville, and the physical resemblance confirms his true identity beyond doubt |
| 5 | Holmes shows Mrs. Laura Lyons a photograph of "Mr. and Mrs. Vandeleur"; she identifies them as Stapleton and Beryl | Ch. 13 / ~86% | External corroboration that definitively confirms Stapleton's double deception — his false promise of marriage to Laura, and his false identity to the world at large |

#### Supporting Clues

| # | Clue | Location (chapter / approx. %) | Notes |
|---|------|-------------------------------|-------|
| 1 | Stapleton knows Grimpen Mire intimately and claims to be the only man who can cross it safely | Ch. 7 / ~37% | Implies he has a strong motive for venturing deep into the mire — where the hound is hidden |
| 2 | At their very first meeting, Stapleton already knows the relationship between Mortimer and Watson, indicating he has done detailed background research on his moorland neighbours | Ch. 7 / ~36% | A "newly arrived" naturalist should not be so well acquainted with the personal connections of people around him |
| 3 | Stapleton behaves contradictorily toward Sir Henry: first he facilitates a courtship between Henry and his "sister," then suddenly erupts with furious opposition to it | Ch. 9 / ~54% | This contradiction can only be explained by a "wife" relationship rather than a "sister" relationship — he is using Beryl to keep watch on Henry, and cannot afford to let her truly leave him |
| 4 | When shadowing Mortimer and Sir Henry in London, Stapleton wears a black false beard and gives the coachman Holmes's name to mislead any pursuit | Ch. 5 / ~31% | Demonstrates considerable intellectual cunning and counter-surveillance skill — an ordinary naturalist would not possess such capabilities |

| # | Clue | Location (chapter / approx. %) | Misleading Direction |
|---|------|-------------------------------|---------------------|
| 1 | Barrymore standing at the window holding a candle, staring out onto the moor in the dead of night; his wife weeping in the small hours | Chs. 6–9 / ~38–57% | Leads the reader to suspect the butler and his wife of involvement in the murder or some conspiracy; in fact they are simply smuggling supplies to the escaped convict Selden — Mrs. Barrymore's brother |
| 2 | The escaped convict Selden — the "Notting Hill murderer" — prowling the moor, violent by nature | Chs. 6–12 / ~33–76% | A conspicuous and threatening figure who diverts the reader's attention away from the far less visible mastermind; his presence is also exploited by Stapleton (Selden is wearing Sir Henry's old clothes and becomes an accidental substitute target for the hound) |
| 3 | The supernatural legend of the "hellhound" haunting the moor | Throughout | Doyle systematically layers a narrative fog — through Mortimer, the local farmers, and regional legend — that makes readers hesitate between "perhaps there is a supernatural force at work" and rational explanation |
| 4 | Old Frankland's telescope — he believes he is tracking the person supplying the escaped convict with food | Ch. 11 / ~70% | What he has actually been watching is Holmes's boy assistant Cartwright, not a child supplying Selden; this scene successfully steers the reader into believing the crucial clue lies in "the direction of the convict" |

### Truth Two: Beryl's True Identity (the inner layer of the whodunit)

- **Clue convergence point: approximately 78%**
- Answer: "Stapleton's sister" Beryl is in fact his wife. Stapleton had his wife pose as his sister so that she could approach Sir Henry as a "free woman" — a tool of manipulation; meanwhile, Stapleton was also deceiving Laura Lyons by promising to marry her.

#### Key Clues

| # | Clue | Location (chapter / approx. %) | Notes |
|---|------|-------------------------------|-------|
| 1 | The very first time Beryl meets Watson, she urgently insists he "return to London immediately" — with an intensity far exceeding the sort of well-meaning warning a friendly neighbour might offer | Ch. 7 / ~38% | A genuine sister would not invest such emotional capital on a first meeting; she knows what "her brother" is doing, and she is crying out with the fear of a wife |
| 2 | When Stapleton intervenes to stop Sir Henry's courtship of Beryl, the fury he displays leaves Henry thoroughly bewildered — "Is he a little mad?" | Ch. 9 / ~55% | A brother's protectiveness of a sister has its limits; ungovernable jealousy is the response of a husband |
| 3 | When Holmes investigates the Vandeleur school, he confirms that "Mr. Vandeleur and his wife" disappeared together | Ch. 12 / ~78% | **The decisive revelation**: Holmes tells Watson directly that "the woman who was presented as Stapleton's sister is in fact his wife" |
| 4 | At the climax, Beryl is found bound to a post in Stapleton's room, with welts from a whip on her neck | Ch. 14 / ~93% | This is domestic violence, not a sibling relationship; a husband punishing his wife for her "betrayal" — because she refused to continue cooperating with the murder plot |

#### Supporting Clues

| # | Clue | Location (chapter / approx. %) | Notes |
|---|------|-------------------------------|-------|
| 1 | Stapleton maintains a secret intimate relationship with Laura Lyons, manipulating her with a promise of marriage into writing the letter that lures Sir Charles | Ch. 12 / ~78% | A single man who has a "sister" developing a quasi-marital relationship with another woman is just barely plausible; but if he is already married, he is conducting matrimonial fraud against two women simultaneously — a moral architecture that is both more complete and more consistent with the comprehensive nature of his scheming |
| 2 | Mortimer has told Watson that Beryl is "an attractive young lady" and that Stapleton brought her to live with him on the moor | Ch. 3 / ~19% | An ambitious man, living alone in an isolated place with a beautiful female companion — this arrangement conforms far more naturally to husband and wife than to brother and sister under the social norms of the time |

#### Red Herrings

| # | Clue | Location (chapter / approx. %) | Misleading Direction |
|---|------|-------------------------------|---------------------|
| 1 | When Stapleton and Beryl first appear, he calls her "Beryl" and she calls him "Jack" — natural, everyday forms of address between family members | Ch. 7 / ~38% | Using familiar first names (rather than "Mr." or "Miss") reinforces the naturalness of the "brother and sister" impression and gives the reader no reason to be suspicious |
| 2 | When Mortimer introduces them to various neighbours, he invariably refers to them as "Mr. Stapleton and his sister"; not a single member of the community entertains any doubt | Various / ~19–50% | The community's collective acceptance creates the strongest possible epistemic barrier — the psychological inertia of "everyone introduces them this way, so it must be fine" |

### Truth Three: The Method of the Crime (howdunit)

- **Clue convergence point: approximately 88%**
- Answer: Stapleton kept a giant hound — a cross between a bloodhound and a mastiff — hidden in the depths of Grimpen Mire. He stole a boot from Sir Henry's hotel to allow the hound to fix upon his scent, then used Laura Lyons to lure Sir Charles to wait alone by the gate of the Yew Alley late at night, before releasing the phosphorus-coated beast. Sir Charles, his heart already weakened, was frightened to death in his flight. Later, Selden — wearing Sir Henry's old clothes — became an accidental victim of the hound. Finally, Sir Henry is manoeuvred into walking alone across the moor, and Holmes shoots the hound dead in the final moments.

#### Key Clues

| # | Clue | Location (chapter / approx. %) | Notes |
|---|------|-------------------------------|-------|
| 1 | Mortimer discovers the pawprint of a gigantic hound near Sir Charles's body but conceals this from the inquest | Chs. 2–3 / ~15% | **The earliest key clue**: confirms that the case involves a real hound and is not simply a case of heart failure |
| 2 | Sir Henry's new boot is stolen at his London hotel — it is later returned, but an old boot disappears in its place | Chs. 4–5 / ~26–30% | A single new boot is of limited use because it carries too little scent. The old boot has been worn by Henry and bears his distinctive smell — this makes it clear that the thief requires a scent marker, that is, an olfactory target for a hound to track |
| 3 | The howling on the moor — Stapleton initially explains it away as "the sound of the bog" or "the cry of a bittern" | Ch. 7 / ~37% | His readiness to dismiss it (and even to proactively offer "rational explanations") is precisely because he knows what the sound is; an innocent person would simply say "I don't know" rather than volunteering multiple reassuring alternatives |
| 4 | Stapleton is the only man who can navigate Grimpen Mire, and there is a locked outbuilding beside his farmhouse | Ch. 7 / ~37% + Ch. 14 / ~91% | The former hints at where the hound is hidden; the latter is confirmed when Watson personally observes Stapleton entering the outbuilding late at night and hears a "strange rustling and stirring" coming from within |
| 5 | Laura Lyons admits that the letter summoning Sir Charles to his appointment — the letter that brought him to the gate — was dictated to her word for word by Stapleton; he arranged both the time and the place of the rendezvous | Ch. 13 / ~85% | Confirms that Stapleton actively staged the scene of Sir Charles's death — it was not a chance encounter, but a carefully designed trap |
| 6 | After the hound is shot dead, Watson touches its muzzle with his fingers; they glow in the dark and carry a distinctive smell | Ch. 14 / ~92% | The confirmation of phosphorus, which explains the "flames of hell" supernatural appearance of the beast — it was chemical camouflage at its core |

#### Supporting Clues

| # | Clue | Location (chapter / approx. %) | Notes |
|---|------|-------------------------------|-------|
| 1 | Sir Charles's footprints change to a "tiptoe" gait after the gate; Holmes correctly deduces that he was running in desperation rather than walking | Ch. 3 / ~18% | The final action before death — a man with a heart condition running in a wild sprint tells us he saw something that drove him to flee in mortal terror |
| 2 | Sir Charles waited at the gate for "more than ten minutes" — the cigar ash fell in two separate deposits — indicating he was waiting for someone, not merely pausing by chance | Ch. 3 / ~17% | Holmes deduces from this that Sir Charles had an appointment, pointing further toward someone deliberately luring him to that spot |
| 3 | Selden, wearing Sir Henry's old clothes, is hunted down and killed by the hound on the moor — confirming that the hound tracks by scent and that it is real | Ch. 12 / ~76% | Indirectly verifies the hound's existence and confirms that the boot theft was intended to establish an olfactory link |
| 4 | Holmes instructs Sir Henry to return home alone along a specific path and arranges an ambush in advance — he is fully confident the hound will appear | Chs. 13–14 / ~87–90% | By this point Holmes has complete command of the howdunit and designs this as the trap to catch Stapleton |

#### Red Herrings

| # | Clue | Location (chapter / approx. %) | Misleading Direction |
|---|------|-------------------------------|---------------------|
| 1 | Sir Charles's death is officially ruled "natural causes (cardiac failure)" by the inquest | Ch. 2 / ~13% | The official verdict creates the legal illusion that "there is no crime," making it difficult for both the reader and the characters to press the case for murder |

## Reasoning Path Reconstruction

**First Path: Who is the killer?**

The earliest point of suspicion arises in Chapter 4 (approximately 28%): Mortimer mentions that Roger Baskerville "died in Central America," but the account is vague and no one confirmed it. An attentive reader should at this point make a mental note that "there is a gap in the family line of inheritance."

By Chapter 7 (approximately 37%), Stapleton has made his entrance. He "happened" to move to the moor shortly after Sir Charles returned home, his knowledge of the area far exceeds what two years of residence would explain, and he is already acquainted with the details of Mortimer's, Watson's, and others' personal relationships. A reader alert to the question "why does this person know so much?" should already be forming suspicions about him.

Chapter 9 (approximately 55%) is a pivotal moment: Stapleton's explosive rage at Henry baffles every neighbour who witnesses it. If he were truly a brother protecting his sister, the behaviour is far beyond the norm; a reader who at this point reasons in the direction of "he is concealing some kind of private relationship" is very close to the truth.

By Chapter 12 (approximately 78%), Holmes reveals to Watson in the stone hut that "Beryl is his wife, not his sister, and he was formerly Vandeleur." This is the convergence point for all the clues the reader has been tracking about Stapleton's identity. The portrait scene in Chapter 13 (approximately 82%) is the final confirmation — the visual identification locks everything into place.

**Second Path: How was it done?**

The stolen boot puzzle (Chapters 4–5, approximately 26–30%) is the earliest and most important howdunit clue in the entire book, but its full significance does not become clear until later. One boot alone is of limited use, but two or more boots carrying scent can be used for tracking — if the reader at this point asks "why would anyone need the smell of a shoe?", then in the midst of the baying on the moor (Chapters 7–9, approximately 37–55%), they may piece together the answer: **someone is using a real hound to track a specific scent target**.

The rustling from the outbuilding (Chapter 14, approximately 91%) is the final piece of the puzzle. The phosphorus revelation (approximately 92%) solves the last riddle of the "luminous hellhound."

**Third Path: The hardest detail to find**

Laura Lyons's role (Chapters 10–13) is the most deeply hidden thread in the book. Her existence reveals that Stapleton was not merely manipulating his wife, but had simultaneously made false promises of marriage to another woman. The design of having two women deceived at the same time presents with stark clarity the essence of Stapleton's character — "everything is a tool, every emotion a lie" — and constitutes one of the most chilling elements of this case in the history of detective fiction.

## Additional Notes

**The unique place of The Hound of the Baskervilles in the Holmes canon**

Doyle had "killed" Holmes in "The Final Problem" (1893), provoking outrage among readers. This novel represents Holmes's return after eight years of silence, framed as a retrospective account of events that took place before the Reichenbach Falls — serialised from 1901. Doyle's choice to welcome readers back with this story was an act of supremely shrewd commercial judgement: *The Hound of the Baskervilles* is simultaneously a Gothic horror novel and a classical mystery, satisfying the expectations of two entirely different audiences.

**The brilliance of the structural design**

The book's greatest narrative achievement is its use of **Watson's letters and diary** in place of direct narration — everything the reader sees is Watson's "real-time record," not a retrospective tidying of memories. This design produces a double effect:
1. It creates immediacy (the reader is confused in real time, in lockstep with Watson)
2. It legitimately conceals the fact that Holmes has already travelled to the moor himself (Holmes's movements are entirely outside Watson's field of vision)

The moment Holmes appears at the stone hut on the moor (Chapter 11) is one of the most celebrated instances in the history of detective fiction of an author deliberately withholding information from the reader. Strictly speaking, Doyle did not "lie" — he simply had Holmes maintain silence about Watson's letters and ensured that Watson did not know where Holmes was.

**A closer look at the Fog Rating**

The reason for four stars rather than five: although the book is rich in red herrings, Doyle does not employ an unreliable narrator or a meta-structural deception, and all of the clues can be found in the text on a rereading. Compared with Christie's five-star fog works (such as *Murder on the Orient Express* or *And Then There Were None*), Doyle's misdirection relies far more on "the emotional impact of individual scenes" than on "a fundamental deception built into the narrative structure."

**A deeper observation on technique**

Stapleton's design as a villain represents a common but underrated theme in Victorian detective fiction: **the corruption of the educator**. He is a schoolmaster — a man who educates and shapes young minds; what he keeps is not a weapon but a natural specimen; his outward appearance is one of scientific rationality. This "savagery beneath a civilised surface" carries in Doyle's hands a clear note of social satire — the most dangerous villains are often those whose appearance is the most reassuring.

The role of Dartmoor in the novel also deserves mention: it is not merely a setting but an active presence. The inscrutability of Grimpen Mire, the stone hut ruins left by prehistoric man, the brutalised existence of the convict lurking in the wilderness — Doyle shapes the moor as the antithesis of civilisation, and every secret in the book is hidden on the other side of that divide. Stapleton's ultimate fate — vanishing into the mire, never to be found — is a justice that borders on the poetic: the chaos of nature swallowing a man who sought to harness the forces of nature in the service of evil.
