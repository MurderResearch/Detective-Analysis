# The Case-Book of Sherlock Holmes — Detective Fiction Analysis

## Basic Information
- Author: Arthur Conan Doyle
- Year: 1927
- Type: Short story collection (12 stories)
- Detective: Sherlock Holmes
- Core mystery types: whodunit / howdunit / whydunit (varies by story)

---

## Deducibility Rating (Entire Collection)

| Metric | Rating | Notes |
|--------|--------|-------|
| Deducibility | ⭐⭐⭐ | Average convergence point ~65% — varies greatly across stories, ranging from moderate to high difficulty; most mysteries require reading more than halfway through before full convergence |
| Fog Rating | ⭐⭐⭐⭐ | Doyle excels at misdirection through "plausible appearances and powerful false leads"; "The Adventure of the Sussex Vampire" nearly reaches the limits of narrative deception |

> **Collection Note:** *The Case-Book of Sherlock Holmes* is the final entry in Doyle's Holmes series, collecting the last 12 short stories published between 1921 and 1927. Doyle personally bid farewell to readers in the preface. This analysis selects the four stories with the greatest depth of deductive design — "The Adventure of the Sussex Vampire," "The Problem of Thor Bridge," "The Adventure of the Three Garridebs," and "The Adventure of the Mazarin Stone" — for complete analysis, along with a collective commentary.

---

## Synopsis

These twelve stories, written in the later years of Doyle's life, bear a noticeably different quality from his early *Adventures*: the cases are more complex, moral contours more ambiguous, and there are occasional passages that fail modern standards of representation (especially the prejudiced portrayal of Black characters in "The Adventure of the Three Gables"). Yet several stories reach the pinnacle of the series in terms of deductive construction.

The twelve stories in order are: (I) The Adventure of the Illustrious Client, (II) The Adventure of the Blanched Soldier, (III) The Adventure of the Mazarin Stone, (IV) The Adventure of the Three Gables, (V) The Adventure of the Sussex Vampire, (VI) The Adventure of the Three Garridebs, (VII) The Problem of Thor Bridge, (VIII) The Adventure of the Creeping Man, (IX) The Adventure of the Lion's Mane, (X) The Adventure of the Veiled Lodger, (XI) The Adventure of Shoscombe Old Place, (XII) The Adventure of the Retired Colourman.

This analysis focuses in depth on the four stories with the most precisely crafted mystery designs.

## Truth Dissection

### Story One: The Adventure of the Sussex Vampire

#### Basic Information
- Core mystery type: whydunit + howdunit
- Surface puzzle: Is the wife a vampire, sucking the blood of her own infant?
- Real puzzle: Who is actually harming the baby? What explains the mother's strange behaviour?

---

#### Deducibility Rating (This Story)

| Metric | Rating | Notes |
|--------|--------|-------|
| Deducibility | ⭐⭐⭐⭐ | Average convergence point ~72% — the true culprit's identity is not fully confirmed until Holmes observes Jack's face reflected in the glass of a window |
| Fog Rating | ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ | The supernatural narrative framework runs throughout from beginning to end: the "vampire" interpretation is the most powerful false lead, one that Holmes himself must actively resist |

---

#### Story Overview (This Story)

Robert Ferguson engages Holmes to investigate a horrifying matter: his Peruvian wife was witnessed by both the nurse and Ferguson himself bending over the infant's neck and sucking blood, with a clear wound visible on the baby's skin. Furthermore, she had on several occasions struck Ferguson's fifteen-year-old disabled son Jack — the child of his first wife — without apparent cause. The wife remains silent about everything, neither explaining herself nor asking forgiveness, saying only that "he should trust me."

The truth: Jack — physically limited by a spinal injury, harbouring an obsessive love for his father — had been secretly wounding the infant using small bird arrows from the South American weapon collection on the wall, arrows tipped with curare poison. The mother had seen through the truth, but chose to personally suck out the poison with her mouth to protect her husband's heart, preferring to be misunderstood as a vampire rather than expose the son her husband loved most.

---

#### Truth One: The Real Source of Danger to the Infant (whodunit)

- **Convergence point: ~72%**
- Answer: Jack used South American poisoned arrows to harm the infant; driven by obsessive love for his father and jealousy toward the newborn, he had acted secretly on multiple occasions.

##### Key Clues

| # | Clue | Location (%) | Notes |
|---|------|-------------|-------|
| 1 | A complete set of South American weapons hangs on the wall, including a blowpipe, a small bird bow, and a quiver | ~52% (when Holmes enters the hall) | Holmes has an "instinctive feeling" about the importance of the weapon — he directly references it in his later reasoning |
| 2 | Holmes notices an empty quiver among the displayed items | ~58% | Someone has taken arrows from it, but no one accounts for where they went — a direct physical clue |
| 3 | The hound Carlo suddenly developed hind-leg paralysis four months prior; the veterinarian could find no cause | ~60% | Holmes immediately says "very suggestive"; implies someone had been "testing" the poison on the dog — a key clue |
| 4 | In the reflection of the window blinds, Holmes sees Jack bending over the infant with an expression of intense jealousy and hatred | ~72% | This is the final key clue; Jack's subjective motive is fully exposed here, and the reasoning converges |

##### Supporting Clues

| # | Clue | Location (%) | Notes |
|---|------|-------------|-------|
| 1 | The wife's "attacks" on Jack always coincide with or follow the incidents of harm to the infant — suggesting the two events are connected | ~35% | Ferguson mentions the timing correlation between the two incidents, but does not himself grasp its significance |
| 2 | Jack is "physically restricted but mentally precocious," displaying an unusual degree of possessiveness toward his father | ~38% | Holmes specifically inquires further, implying Jack's psychology is the core of the mystery |
| 3 | The wife says "he should trust me" and firmly refuses to explain why | ~55% | A guilty person would not speak this way — she has something she cannot say, rather than nothing to say |
| 4 | After privately whispering with the nurse, Holmes tells her "your anxiety will soon be removed" | ~68% | The nurse clearly holds certain information; Holmes has already grasped the full picture |

##### False Clues

| # | Clue | Location (%) | Misleading Direction |
|---|------|-------------|---------------------|
| 1 | The nurse witnesses the wife bending over the infant, blood at her mouth, with a wound on the baby's neck | Throughout | All observers (including Holmes's case index, Ferguson's account) point toward the wife as the perpetrator |
| 2 | Holmes consults the "vampires" index, confirming the folklore | ~5% | Doyle deliberately has Holmes briefly "consider" supernatural possibilities, raising the ceiling of the reader's assumptions |
| 3 | The wife refuses to explain herself, lies in her room with a low fever refusing to see her husband, behaving "as if guilty" | ~55–65% | Silence is interpreted as inability to defend herself; in reality she is protecting Jack |
| 4 | The wife has struck Jack on multiple occasions — implying she is hostile to the child | ~30–40% | Leads the reader to believe the wife is the aggressor against Jack; in reality she is "retaliating" against Jack's secret attacks |

---

#### Truth Two: The Wife's True Actions (howdunit)

- **Convergence point: ~60%**
- Answer: The wife witnessed Jack use a poisoned arrow to wound the infant, and immediately sucked out the curare with her mouth to prevent the toxin from spreading. She chose silence because she could not reveal the truth without destroying her husband's mind.

##### Key Clues

| # | Clue | Location (%) | Notes |
|---|------|-------------|-------|
| 1 | The wound on the infant's neck is described as "a small puckered mark" | ~55% | Unlike a bite wound; more like a puncture. Holmes examines it carefully without comment, but with a serious demeanour |
| 2 | The South American tradition of sucking out poison with one's mouth (and the English historical precedent of a queen sucking a wound to draw out venom) | ~75% (during Holmes's reasoning) | Holmes says "Has no one thought that sucking the wound might have another purpose?" — this is the core reasoning reversal |

---

#### Reasoning Path Reconstruction (The Adventure of the Sussex Vampire)

At around **5%**, Holmes declares his position with the maxim "No ghosts need apply": this case must have a rational explanation. The careful reader should follow this conviction and begin thinking under the premise that "a vampire is impossible."

At around **52–58%**, as Holmes enters the hall, Doyle devotes a passage to describing the South American weapon collection, specifically calling out the **empty quiver**. This is a rare moment of an "exposed clue" — the author does not hide it, and readers who read carefully should form a hypothesis here.

At around **60%**, the hound Carlo's sudden paralysis is raised, and Holmes immediately says it is "very suggestive" — a confirmation signal from Doyle to the reader: the weapons, the empty quiver, the sick dog — three threads have begun to converge.

At around **72%**, Holmes observes Jack's expression in the reflected glass — the final confirmation point. By this stage, a careful reader should be able to complete the reasoning.

However, while "who did it" can be known by this point, the motivational mystery of "why the mother stayed silent" is not fully resolved until Holmes explains it directly after **80%**.

### Story Two: The Problem of Thor Bridge

#### Basic Information
- Core mystery type: howdunit + whydunit (suicide staged as murder)
- Surface puzzle: Did governess Grace Dunbar murder her employer's wife?
- Real puzzle: How did Maria Gibson die on the bridge without leaving behind a weapon?

---

#### Deducibility Rating (This Story)

| Metric | Rating | Notes |
|--------|--------|-------|
| Deducibility | ⭐⭐⭐⭐ | Average convergence point ~78% — the core physical clue (the chip in the bridge railing) does not appear until later in the on-site investigation |
| Fog Rating | ⭐⭐⭐⭐ | The case itself is a perfect frame-up: evidence, motive, and opportunity all point to an innocent person |

---

#### Story Overview (This Story)

Neil Gibson, a gold mining magnate, finds his Brazilian wife Maria shot through the head by a revolver bullet late at night on Thor Bridge, with no weapon found near the body. Yet in the wardrobe of Grace Dunbar — the young, beautiful governess he employed — a revolver is found, its calibre matching the fatal bullet; moreover, a handwritten note in Dunbar's writing is found in Maria's pocket, inviting her to meet on the bridge that night; and a passing villager had seen Dunbar on the bridge.

The truth: Maria, knowing her husband had fallen in love with Dunbar, chose to take her own life — but devised a perfect mechanism. She tied the revolver to a stone with a rope, so that when she pulled the trigger, the weight of the stone would pull the revolver into the water below the bridge — staging her death as murder, so that Dunbar would be sentenced to death, completing her ultimate act of revenge.

---

#### Truth: Maria's Mechanical Suicide and Framing Plan

- **Convergence point: ~78%**
- Answer: Maria Gibson designed a "disappearing weapon" mechanism — she tied the revolver to a stone, with one end fixed to the bridge railing. After firing, the weight of the stone would pull the revolver into the water. She left an identifiable **chip mark** on the bridge railing — the only physical evidence of the mechanism after the weapon disappeared.

##### Key Clues

| # | Clue | Location (%) | Notes |
|---|------|-------------|-------|
| 1 | Holmes repeatedly emphasises "No weapon was found near her" — the way he pauses when saying this is unusual | ~15% | Doyle signals through Holmes's tone: "no weapon" is not an ordinary statement, but the core of the mystery |
| 2 | The revolver found in the wardrobe was "lying on the floor" rather than hidden | ~20% | Holmes repeats this detail three times with increasing emphasis — a genuine killer would not hide a weapon this way |
| 3 | A **fresh chip** (a chip in the stone) and marks from a rough cord are found on the stone railing of Thor Bridge | ~78% | This is the final decisive clue — the physical trace of the mechanism; reasoning fully converges here |
| 4 | Holmes finds the missing revolver in the lake mud beneath the bridge, with the stone still tied to it | ~82% | Final confirmation, but readers should in theory be able to deduce the mechanism upon seeing the chip |

##### Supporting Clues

| # | Clue | Location (%) | Notes |
|---|------|-------------|-------|
| 1 | Maria was a "tropical" Brazilian woman of extreme passions, who "loved him as such women can love" | ~55% | Establishes the motivational basis — such love, when turned to despair, can become extreme action |
| 2 | Gibson admits he fell in love with Dunbar and had declared his feelings to her | ~60% | Maria must have known — she had ample motive to devise the framing |
| 3 | Dunbar says she went to Thor Bridge because she received a letter requesting a meeting (in Maria's own handwriting) | ~70% | Maria designed the entire encounter — she ensured Dunbar would be present at the scene |

##### False Clues

| # | Clue | Location (%) | Misleading Direction |
|---|------|-------------|---------------------|
| 1 | The revolver found on the wardrobe floor (calibre matches) | ~10% | Points directly at Dunbar and was found by a third party, appearing to be objective evidence |
| 2 | The handwritten letter from Maria inviting Dunbar to the bridge — whether forged in Dunbar's name or genuinely sent | ~15% | Leads a jury to believe Dunbar "premeditated" luring the victim to the murder scene |
| 3 | Gibson's cruel character and indifference toward his wife — implying he may be the mastermind | ~40% | The estate manager Bates emphasises Gibson's "brutality," directing suspicion toward Gibson |
| 4 | Dunbar cannot provide an alibi, and a villager witnessed her on the bridge | ~20% | Makes Dunbar's self-defence appear weak and unconvincing |

---

#### Reasoning Path Reconstruction (The Problem of Thor Bridge)

At around **15%**, the way Holmes pauses when saying "no weapon was found near her" should alert readers that this is a deliberately marked puzzle point.

At around **20%**, Holmes emphasises three times that the revolver was on the "floor" of the wardrobe — if the murderer were Dunbar, such a hiding place would make no sense. Careful readers should begin at this point to suspect Dunbar has been framed.

But to answer "where did the weapon go" and "how did the killer dispose of it," one must wait until the on-site investigation reveals the **fresh chip** on the bridge railing (at ~78%) — this is the final piece of the complete reasoning path.

The path from "no weapon" → "the weapon must be in the water" → "how did the weapon get into the water" → "the stone mechanism" — Doyle deliberately withholds any direct clue to the "physical mechanism" before the reader has a chance to reason it out independently. Only that chip is the observable original trace. This is the most refined howdunit design in the entire collection.

### Story Three: The Adventure of the Three Garridebs

#### Basic Information
- Core mystery type: whodunit + whydunit (the true purpose of the scheme)
- Surface puzzle: Is the "Garrideb" inheritance case genuine?
- Real puzzle: Why does "Killer" Evans go to such elaborate lengths to construct this absurd name-search hoax?

---

#### Deducibility Rating (This Story)

| Metric | Rating | Notes |
|--------|--------|-------|
| Deducibility | ⭐⭐⭐ | Average convergence point ~55% — that Evans is a fraud can be confirmed fairly early, but his true purpose is not established until the second half |
| Fog Rating | ⭐⭐⭐ | The scheme itself is cleverly designed, but the cracks in the lie appear relatively early; the difficulty lies mainly in "why the deception" rather than "who is deceiving" |

---

#### Story Overview (This Story)

An American calling himself "John Garrideb, Counsellor at Law, Topeka, Kansas" approaches Nathan Garrideb in London, claiming that a wealthy American named Alexander Hamilton Garrideb left a will stating that if three men of the same surname can be found, each shall inherit five million dollars. Nathan, eager to find a third Garrideb, turns to Holmes for help.

The truth: "John Garrideb" is actually Killer Evans (real name James Winter, alias Killer Evans). The purpose of his scheme is not wealth, but to get the stay-at-home collector Nathan out of his flat — because beneath the flat's floorboards lies a printing press and two thousand forged hundred-pound banknotes left by Prescott, the victim of a previous murder Evans committed. Prescott had been a brilliant counterfeiter. Evans killed Prescott at this address and now wants to return to retrieve the fortune.

---

#### Truth: The Real Purpose of the Scheme (whydunit)

- **Convergence point: ~55%**
- Answer: Evans wants the counterfeiting equipment and forged banknotes hidden beneath the flat's floor. The previous tenant Prescott — his murder victim — had concealed a printing press here, and Evans knows the location but cannot access it because Nathan never leaves his flat.

##### Key Clues

| # | Clue | Location (%) | Notes |
|---|------|-------------|-------|
| 1 | "John Garrideb's" coat and trousers are heavily worn — nothing like the dress of "a traveller just arrived from America" | ~18% | Holmes says "I was on the verge of asking him: what are you hiding?" but chooses to continue feigning trust — the clothing is an early signal of deception |
| 2 | The newspaper advertisement columns have never shown any "Garrideb" personal notice — despite Evans claiming he had advertised for some time | ~30% | Holmes specifically says: "The agony columns are my specialty; I never miss them." Directly punctures the lie |
| 3 | Holmes invents "Dr. Lysander Starr of Topeka" — Evans says "Good old fellow!" | ~35% | Holmes's test trap: this person never existed; Evans "recognising" him proves he is lying |
| 4 | Confirmation of the identity of previous tenant Waldron / Prescott; Prescott had once lived at this address | ~55% | Connects the murder case, the counterfeiter, and this address — the true purpose of the scheme converges here |

##### Supporting Clues

| # | Clue | Location (%) | Notes |
|---|------|-------------|-------|
| 1 | On learning that Holmes has become involved, Evans "becomes angry" — but tells Nathan "Holmes is all right, I just want to do this directly" — an abrupt change in attitude | ~38% | The emotional shift is unnatural; he needs to keep the scheme running |
| 2 | The advertisement's English spelling (plough spelled as plow, buckboard, etc.) is American English | ~65% | The advertisement was placed by Evans himself; he ensured he could gain access on the day Nathan would leave |
| 3 | Nathan has absolutely no desire to leave his collection room; all of Evans's efforts are focused on "getting this man out the door" | Throughout | The only motive is needing the flat temporarily vacated |

##### False Clues

| # | Clue | Location (%) | Misleading Direction |
|---|------|-------------|---------------------|
| 1 | The complete legal details of "Alexander Hamilton Garrideb's will" | ~15% | Draws the reader's focus to "is the inheritance case legitimate?" rather than "why create this inheritance case?" |
| 2 | Evans actively produces the advertisement for "Howard Garrideb," appearing to sincerely advance the plan | ~60% | Makes the reader think he is genuinely searching for Garridebs; in reality, the advertisement was forged by himself |

---

#### Reasoning Path Reconstruction (The Adventure of the Three Garridebs)

At around **18%**, the worn clothing provides the first signal. At around **30–35%**, the advertising lie and the Lysander Starr test establish the proposition "Evans is a fraud" as essentially certain.

But "why the deception?" is the story's true mystery. Someone deceiving purely for financial gain has no need for such a complex scheme — he could simply steal or threaten. What compels him to go to such lengths to drive an old man who never goes out from his flat?

At around **55%**, the identity of former tenant Prescott is confirmed, and the hypothesis "something is hidden here" becomes formally established. The subsequent action merely confirms this.

The story's most moving scene is not the triumph of deduction, but the moment after Evans shoots Watson and Holmes's humanity flashes through — "If you had killed Watson, you would not have left this room alive" — the least guarded moment in the entire series for Holmes.

### Story Four: The Adventure of the Mazarin Stone

#### Basic Information
- Core mystery type: howdunit (how the detective retrieves the jewel from a dangerous situation)
- Surface puzzle: Can Holmes recover the Crown diamond from an assassin who plans to murder him?
- Real puzzle: How does Holmes, while ostensibly "in his bedroom," learn where the diamond is hidden?

---

#### Deducibility Rating (This Story)

| Metric | Rating | Notes |
|--------|--------|-------|
| Deducibility | ⭐⭐⭐ | Average convergence point ~65% — the wax dummy and the gramophone are each mentioned at the start of the story, but the complete design of how Holmes uses them is not revealed until the end |
| Fog Rating | ⭐⭐⭐ | Doyle directly informs the reader "there is a dummy, there is a sniper, there is an assassin coming to visit" — the mystery lies not in "who" but in "how it was done" |

---

#### Story Overview (This Story)

Holmes is investigating Count Sylvius for the theft of the Mazarin Stone — the Crown diamond. The Count, with murder in mind, personally calls at Baker Street — he intends to ascertain how much Holmes knows before deciding whether to eliminate him. Holmes admits the Count into what appears to be an "empty room" with a wax dummy in Holmes's dressing gown seated in the corner, while Holmes himself listens through a second concealed door from his bedroom as the Count converses with his accomplice Sam Merton — the Count, unaware he is being overheard, reveals to his associate that the diamond is concealed in a "secret pocket" on his person.

This is one of the few stories in the collection where "how the detective acts" is the core puzzle, full of ingenious wit — but the reader's participatory role in the deduction is relatively limited.

---

#### Truth: Holmes's Double Mechanism

- **Convergence point: ~65%**
- Answer: Holmes uses a gramophone playing violin music (making the criminals believe he is practising in his bedroom), slips through the concealed door to hide behind the curtains and eavesdrop, while simultaneously using the wax dummy to deceive the Count's accomplice.

##### Key Clues

| # | Clue | Location (%) | Notes |
|---|------|-------------|-------|
| 1 | Billy points out the "wax dummy" behind the curtain — identical in appearance to Holmes, adjustable in angle | ~8% | The existence of the dummy is clearly disclosed to the reader from the outset; the reader knows Holmes is setting a trap, but does not know the complete plan |
| 2 | Holmes tells Watson "there is a second door to the bedroom leading behind those curtains" | ~40% (revealed near the end) | Doyle chooses by design not to disclose the concealed door in advance, allowing this to be the final magical reveal |
| 3 | The gramophone plays Hoffmann's Barcarolle — Sam says "that violin music is getting on my nerves" | ~65% | The music is the pillar of the illusion that "Holmes is in his bedroom"; the gramophone was common technology by 1927, but the reader's attention is redirected |

##### Supporting Clues

| # | Clue | Location (%) | Notes |
|---|------|-------------|-------|
| 1 | The Count says the diamond is "in a secret pocket on my person; I never leave it outside" | ~70% | The Count, unaware he is being monitored, voluntarily reveals the diamond's location to his associate |
| 2 | The Count has his accomplice take the diamond "to the window to see it clearly" — at which point Holmes springs out | ~78% | Holmes waits for this moment; "needing light to see the diamond clearly" is a predictable behaviour |

##### False Clues

| # | Clue | Location (%) | Misleading Direction |
|---|------|-------------|---------------------|
| 1 | Holmes says "show the Count up; I will not be in the room" — the reader assumes he is hiding to avoid danger | ~40% | In reality, he wants the Count to relax his guard in the "empty room" |
| 2 | Holmes sends Watson to fetch the police, "let me first speak with him alone to find out where the diamond is" | ~42% | Implies Holmes plans to use negotiation or intimidation to make the Count reveal the location; the actual method is eavesdropping |

---

#### Reasoning Path Reconstruction (The Adventure of the Mazarin Stone)

The deductive pleasure of this story lies mainly in "piecing together Holmes's design" rather than traditional mystery-solving.

At around **8%**, the dummy is exhibited — a functional prop, and the reader knows it will certainly be put to use. At around **40%**, Holmes dismisses Watson, saying "I only need a few minutes alone with the Count" — the "few minutes" framing implies he intends some form of rapid action, rather than a prolonged negotiation.

The final magical reveal lies in this: the Count and his accomplice believe "music = Holmes in the bedroom," but careful readers should realise, once the Count and his accomplice begin speaking freely in what they believe to be an "unoccupied room," that someone is listening — the only question is "from where."

## Reasoning Path Reconstruction (General Overview of the Collection)

*The Case-Book of Sherlock Holmes*, as the final chapter of Doyle's Holmes series, exhibits several distinctive patterns of deductive construction:

**1. Doyle's "Exposed Clue" Strategy**

In this period, Doyle developed a mature technique: rather than hiding clues, he "displays them while distracting the reader's attention." The South American weapon collection in "The Adventure of the Sussex Vampire" is never concealed; "no weapon" is repeatedly emphasised in "The Problem of Thor Bridge" — but Doyle knows that readers, caught up in the emotional impact of "the vampire" or "the beautiful governess," will often not pause to ask what these details mean.

**2. Emotional Impact as the Most Powerful False Lead**

The most effective misdirection in several stories of this collection is not a carefully designed red herring, but the emotional intensity itself. The supernatural terror of blood-sucking in "The Adventure of the Sussex Vampire," the romantic entanglement between Gibson and Dunbar in "The Problem of Thor Bridge" — these powerful emotional focal points keep readers fixated on the wrong mystery.

**3. The Detective's Knowledge Advantage**

A characteristic of the later Holmes stories is that their solutions often depend on specific specialist knowledge: "The Adventure of the Sussex Vampire" requires knowing the properties of curare, and "The Problem of Thor Bridge" requires an intuitive grasp of ballistic mechanics. Doyle sometimes drew the criticism of "unfair information" for these choices, but such designs also lend the revelations a more striking sense of surprise.

## Additional Notes

**The Place of *The Case-Book* in the Series**

In his preface, Doyle candidly admits that he felt fatigued writing these stories — "like an old tenor who is still forced by his audience to return for encore after encore." The collection is indeed quite uneven in quality: the portrayal of Black characters in "The Adventure of the Three Gables" makes modern readers uncomfortable, the science-fiction premise of "The Adventure of the Creeping Man" is somewhat absurd, and "The Adventure of the Veiled Lodger" and "The Adventure of the Retired Colourman" tend toward the pedestrian.

Yet three stories within reach the most refined deductive designs of Doyle's entire career:

| Story | Most Remarkable Achievement |
|-------|----------------------------|
| The Adventure of the Sussex Vampire | The astonishing reveal of the true culprit (a disabled child), coupled with the perfect disguise of a supernatural exterior |
| The Problem of Thor Bridge | The "disappearing weapon" mechanical design — physical ingenuity in howdunit that has never been surpassed |
| The Adventure of the Three Garridebs | The intricate design of the hidden motive, combined with the most moving moment of Holmes–Watson friendship in the series |

**The Collection's Influence on Subsequent Detective Fiction**

The "disappearing weapon" mechanism of "The Problem of Thor Bridge" is regarded as an important forerunner of the locked-room / disappearance subgenre, directly influencing the later Japanese honkaku mystery tradition (Japanese mystery writers have studied this story with particular care). "The Adventure of the Sussex Vampire's" "least likely suspect" (a disabled child) had a traceable influence on Agatha Christie's thinking when selecting unexpected culprits — Christie acknowledged that she had read extensively in the Holmes canon.

**The Evolution of Doyle's Late Style**

Comparing the early "A Scandal in Bohemia" (1891) or "The Final Problem" (1893), the later Holmes is noticeably more "human": his reaction to Watson being shot ("The Adventure of the Three Garridebs"), his anger on behalf of victimised women (his moral condemnation in "The Adventure of the Illustrious Client"), his remark at the close of "The Adventure of the Sussex Vampire" — "Let them find their own solution; it is time for us to leave" — these are responses that the cool, machine-like early Holmes would never have had. In bidding farewell, Doyle gave the most famous figure of his pen a little of his own warmth.

**A Note for Taiwanese Readers**

Readers in Taiwan encountering this collection for the first time are recommended to read "The Adventure of the Sussex Vampire" before "The Problem of Thor Bridge" — the former demonstrates how Doyle wraps a rational puzzle in a supernatural exterior, while the latter demonstrates how he designs a purely physical mechanical mystery. Reading the two together provides an intuitive sense of the full range of Doyle's deductive methods.
